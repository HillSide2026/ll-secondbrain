# Risk Profile: Business Acquisition

---

## Default Risk Posture

Risk increases with deal size. Level 1 deals carry process and advisory risk; Level 2 and 3 deals carry full transactional risk. Diligence scope, APA protections, and escalation sensitivity should scale accordingly.

---

## Key Failure Surfaces

| Failure | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Incorrect deal level classification | Wrong pathway; under-scoped diligence or documentation |
| PPSA security interests not identified or discharged | Buyer acquires encumbered assets |
| HST s. 167 election missed or defective | Unexpected HST liability on purchase price |
| Lease assignment consent not obtained before closing | Buyer has no right to occupy premises |
| Assumed employees not properly transitioned | ESA liability; wrongful dismissal exposure |
| Regulatory licence not assignable or not transferred | Business cannot legally operate post-closing |
| CRA liabilities not surfaced in diligence | Buyer assumes undisclosed tax exposure (share purchase) |
| Earnout mechanics ambiguous | Disputes on post-closing payments |
| VTB default enforcement not addressed | Seller remedies unclear on buyer default |

---

## Level-Specific Risk Flags

### Level 1 (< $250,000 — Agent-Prepared APA)

- Agent-prepared APAs frequently omit: representations and warranties, material adverse change conditions, PPSA discharge obligations, HST election provisions
- Lawyer is in advisory role only — client may proceed on APA as-is despite legal advice; document advice given
- Deal size may not justify cost of full diligence; ensure proportionate scope is agreed and documented
- Closing may proceed informally; confirm conditions are tracked even in an informal process

### Level 2 ($250,000–$1,000,000 — Lawyer-Prepared APA)

- Full APA scope; representations and warranties, indemnities, and conditions are material
- Diligence should at minimum cover: PPSA, corporate status, material contracts, licences and permits, lease, employees, tax status
- Counterparty is often unrepresented or informally represented — confirm whether counterparty has legal representation at outset
- Payment structure risk: confirm whether any portion is deferred and document security or conditions on deferred amounts

### Level 3 (> $1,000,000 — LOI + Lawyer-Prepared APA)

- LOI: confirm non-binding status is explicit; binding provisions (exclusivity, confidentiality, costs) must be clearly identified
- Full diligence scope including financial due diligence (accountant's role), legal, operational, and regulatory
- Earnout provisions: definitions of revenue/EBITDA metrics, post-closing conduct obligations, buyer non-interference — all require careful drafting
- VTB financing: security instrument required; confirm priority, default provisions, and enforcement rights
- Representations and warranties: survival periods, indemnity caps and baskets, and knowledge qualifiers are all negotiation points requiring ML1 judgment
- Post-closing adjustments: working capital adjustments require clear reference balance sheet and defined methodology

## Non-Obvious Risks

| Risk | Why It Matters |
|------|----------------|
| Informal seller representations relied upon but not in APA | Not enforceable; no contractual remedy |
| Business licence not in buyer's name before opening | Regulatory violation from day one of operation |
| Employees assume continuity of service; buyer disputes | ESA deemed continuity provisions may apply regardless of asset structure |
| Going concern election (s. 167) not filed by closing date | Strict CRA deadline; cannot be retroactively cured |
| Key person dependency not addressed in transition | Business value degrades post-closing without transition provisions |
| Supplier contracts not assignable without consent | Core supply relationships terminate on assignment |

---

## Process Risks

- Conditions to closing not tracked systematically — closing proceeds with unsatisfied conditions
- Post-closing checklist items treated as administrative only — regulatory gaps (licences, HST, PPSA) create live exposure
- Deal urgency overrides diligence completeness — escalation conditions missed under time pressure
- Level misclassification at intake — wrong pathway applied throughout

---

## Escalation Principle

When facts exceed standard assumptions, escalate rather than adapt silently.

Adaptation without escalation creates hidden risk that surfaces after closing, when contractual remedies may be limited.
