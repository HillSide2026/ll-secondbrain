# LLPRN-16 - Regulatory Mechanism Design

Title: Regulatory Mechanism Design

Statement:
When a private relationship or negotiation has a bad equilibrium — where cheating is the dominant strategy — transform the payoff matrix by introducing external enforcement. Contracts with teeth, regulatory bodies, or court intervention can convert defection from a dominant strategy into a costly one, making cooperation the rational choice.

Rationale:
In mechanism design and contract theory, a third-party enforcer changes the game by imposing external penalties on defection. Without enforcement, the Nash Equilibrium of many negotiating situations is mutual defection (both parties exploit each other). With external enforcement, the payoff to defection drops below the payoff to cooperation, shifting the equilibrium. In legal practice this is one of the lawyer's primary tools: structure agreements with real penalties, invoke regulatory oversight when private remedies are inadequate, and use the courts to make the cost of cheating concrete. The lawyer who can credibly threaten external enforcement — and follow through — changes the counterparty's incentive structure.

Application Notes:
1. Mechanism selection: choose the enforcement mechanism that most efficiently changes the payoff matrix. Litigation is expensive for both sides; a regulatory complaint may be faster and more targeted; well-drafted contractual penalties may deter without any proceeding. Match the mechanism to the deterrence need, not to the most aggressive available option.
2. Threat as mechanism: the credible threat of external enforcement is often more powerful than the enforcement itself, because the target also bears costs from the process regardless of outcome. The threat functions only if it is credible (see LLPRN-14). An empty threat of litigation trains the counterparty to disregard the next one.
