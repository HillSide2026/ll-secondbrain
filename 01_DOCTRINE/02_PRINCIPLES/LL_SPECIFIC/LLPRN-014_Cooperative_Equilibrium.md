# LLPRN-014 - Cooperative Equilibrium

Title: Cooperative Equilibrium

Statement:
In repeated professional engagements, the long-term value of sustained cooperation exceeds the short-term gain of defection. Prefer tacit coordination with reliable counterparties to reach mutually beneficial outcomes. Defection destroys the cooperative surplus; cooperation compounds it.

Rationale:
In game theory, cooperation in a Prisoner's Dilemma is unstable in a one-shot game but sustainable in a repeated (infinite-horizon) game, because both players know that defection today triggers retaliation tomorrow, eliminating all future cooperative gains. In legal practice this applies to ongoing relationships with opposing counsel, referral sources, and professional counterparties. Short-term extraction (overreaching in negotiation, burning a colleague, taking advantage of an information asymmetry) may produce a one-time gain but destroys the repeated-game surplus. The correct posture in a long-horizon relationship is to treat the cooperation itself as the asset worth protecting.

Note: Cooperation here does not mean capitulation or collusion in the pejorative sense. It means recognizing that in long-term relationships, mutual restraint produces better aggregate outcomes than mutual defection.

Application Notes:
1. Last-period recognition: cooperative equilibrium holds only when both players value future payoffs. A counterparty who knows the relationship is ending — a client near the close of their matter, opposing counsel retiring, a contact leaving the jurisdiction — is in a last-period game. Defection becomes rational for them regardless of history. Recognize these situations and adjust expectations; do not rely on cooperative norms that have no future to sustain them.
2. New counterparties: with a counterparty whose type is unknown, cooperation has not yet been established. The first few interactions are signal-gathering, not full cooperation. Do not extend full cooperative trust before the pattern has been observed.
