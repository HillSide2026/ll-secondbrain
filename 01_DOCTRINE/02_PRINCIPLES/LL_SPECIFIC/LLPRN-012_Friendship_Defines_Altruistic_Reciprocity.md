# LLPRN-012 - Friendship Defines Altruistic Reciprocity

Title: Friendship Defines Altruistic Reciprocity

Statement:
Only friends get altruistic reciprocity. Altruistic reciprocity means giving, supporting, or advocating motivated by genuine care for the other person — not by expectation of proportional return, and not by strategic calculation. This is not the same as unconditional giving: altruistic reciprocity is grounded in the friendship relationship and does not extend beyond it. Colleagues and clients do not get altruistic reciprocity regardless of how warm or productive the relationship becomes. Good colleagues get quid pro quo. Clients get commercial engagement. The motivation and the category are different things.

Rationale:
Altruistic reciprocity is distinct from both unconditional giving and from quid pro quo. Unconditional giving has no relational basis — you give to anyone regardless of relationship. Quid pro quo is transactional — you give because you expect proportional return. Altruistic reciprocity is neither: it is relational and genuine but not calculated. It exists only within a real friendship, where both people care about each other, and that is precisely what makes it appropriate in that category and inappropriate in the colleague or client categories. Extending it to colleagues or clients does not make those relationships warmer — it corrupts the reciprocity norms that make them function. A colleague who receives altruistic support has no reason to maintain the good-colleague standard; the signal is gone.

Application Notes:
1. Friend classification: friendship is evidenced by mutual off-the-clock investment, genuine personal disclosure, and behavior in adversity — not by warmth or professional pleasantness alone. A warm, reliable good colleague is not thereby a friend.
2. Asymmetric classification: the most expensive misclassification is treating someone as a friend who treats you as a good colleague. This asymmetry is typically invisible until a moment of real cost makes it apparent. Assess whether the other person would describe the relationship the same way you would.
3. Transition: a good colleague can become a friend, but the shift requires the professional relationship to wind down. Holding both simultaneously corrupts the reciprocity norms of each (see LLPRN-009, LLPRN-004).
