# LLPRN-14 - Credible Commitment

Title: Credible Commitment

Statement:
When making a strategic threat or promise, limit your own future options to make the commitment credible. A threat that preserves a retreat option is a bluff. A commitment that eliminates retreat is a rational deterrent, because the counterparty knows you will follow through regardless of cost.

Rationale:
In non-cooperative game theory, a "commitment device" changes the player's own future incentives to make a threat or promise believable. If you cannot retreat, the threat to fight to the end becomes the only rational move — and the counterparty knows it. In legal practice, this applies to litigation posture, settlement negotiations, and professional boundary enforcement. A lawyer who routinely backs down from stated positions trains counterparties to test them. A lawyer who commits in ways that make retreat costly (filing, public position, client instruction) forces the counterparty to take the position seriously.

Counterpart strategies: See LLPRN-17 (Credible Retaliation) for the enforcement side. Commitment governs what you say you will do; retaliation governs what you actually do when tested.

Application Notes:
1. Signaling cost: a commitment is credible only if it is costly to make and costly to reverse. Cheap statements of intent are not commitments. The more it costs you to back down, the more credible the commitment to the counterparty.
2. Partial commitment trap: a halfway commitment ("I might do X") is worse than either full commitment or silence. It signals uncertainty and invites testing. If you are not prepared to commit fully, do not partially commit.
3. Retainer as commitment device: see LLPRN-07. Requiring a retainer before proceeding is itself a mutual commitment mechanism — it establishes financial skin-in-the-game on both sides and makes the commercial character of the relationship explicit from the outset.
