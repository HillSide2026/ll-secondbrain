# Financial Services Law — Foundational Library

> **Research grade — not approved. Not to be treated as authoritative.**
> This document catalogs foundational academic and secondary literature for the
> Financial Services / Payments practice area. Sources are indexed by category
> with citation, relevance notes, and retrieval status.

---

## Purpose

This library supplements the RPAA and CARF syntheses. It provides the doctrinal,
comparative, and academic foundation for financial services law practice —
covering payment systems law, AML/MSB doctrine, commercial payment instruments,
payments infrastructure, and policy literature on fintech and crypto regulation.

---

## 1. Core Canadian Banking and Payments Treatises

### 1.1 Banking and Financial Institutions Law in Canada

**Citation:** Ziegel, J. S., Denomme, D., & Lederman, B. (loose-leaf, updated).
*Banking and Financial Institutions Law in Canada.* Toronto: LexisNexis Canada.

**Relevance:** The leading Canadian banking law treatise. Covers the full spectrum
of banking relationships, payment systems, and clearing. Required reading for
understanding the legal architecture within which RPAA, PCMLTFA, and payment
rail access obligations sit.

**Key coverage:** Payment systems law, clearing and settlement, bank-customer
relationships, bank regulatory framework, deposit-taking institutions.

**Retrieval:** Print / LexisNexis Canada (loose-leaf). Not available online.
Firm library access required.

---

### 1.2 The Law of Banking and Financial Institutions in Canada (2nd ed.)

**Citation:** Crawford, B., & Falzon, N. (2019). *The Law of Banking and
Financial Institutions in Canada* (2nd ed.). Toronto: LexisNexis Canada.

**Relevance:** More modern and structured treatment than Ziegel. Useful for
regulatory architecture and payments context. Second edition incorporates
developments through approximately 2018 — predates RPAA but provides the
foundational framework.

**Key coverage:** Banking regulation, payment systems, financial institution
law, regulatory compliance structure.

**Retrieval:** Print / LexisNexis Canada. Not available online.

---

### 1.3 Banking Law in Canada (Ogilvie — payment systems chapters)

**Citation:** Ogilvie, M. H. (various editions). *Banking Law in Canada*
(relevant chapters on payment systems). Toronto: Carswell.

**Relevance:** One of the few doctrinal Canadian sources on payment mechanics.
The payment systems chapters provide foundational treatment of the legal rules
governing funds transfers, clearing obligations, and payment finality in Canada.
Not a standalone payments treatise — value is in the specific chapters.

**Retrieval:** Print / WestlawNext Canada (Carswell). Not available online.

## 2. AML / MSB — Core Practitioner Text

### 2.1 Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing

**Citation:** German, P. (latest ed.). *Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering)
and Terrorist Financing.* Toronto: Thomson Reuters (Carswell).

**Relevance:** The definitive Canadian practitioner text on PCMLTFA. Critical
for MSB qualification analysis, payment intermediary obligations, reporting
entity determination, compliance program requirements, and enforcement. Should
be the primary reference for any PCMLTFA advisory work — including MSB
registration, effectiveness reviews, and STR matters.

**Key coverage:** PCMLTFA structure, MSB definition and categories, AML/KYC
program requirements, FINTRAC obligations, STR/LVTR/EFTR reporting, penalties,
criminal liability, effectiveness review.

**Retrieval:** Print / WestlawNext Canada (Carswell). Not available online.
Confirm current edition.

## 3. Payment Instruments and Commercial Law

### 3.1 The Law of Bills of Exchange, Cheques and Promissory Notes

**Citation:** Ogden, J. A. (various editions). *The Law of Bills of Exchange,
Cheques and Promissory Notes.* Toronto: Carswell.

**Relevance:** Legacy foundational text on payment instruments and transfer
obligations. Although predating modern electronic payments, it remains the
doctrinal baseline for understanding payment obligations, negotiability,
and holder-in-due-course rules. Relevant for understanding the legal nature
of payment finality and instrument discharge.

**Key coverage:** Bills of exchange, cheques, promissory notes, transfer
obligations, discharge.

**Retrieval:** Print / WestlawNext Canada (Carswell). Not available online.

---

### 3.2 Canadian Commercial Law Guide

**Citation:** McGuinness, K. (loose-leaf). *Canadian Commercial Law Guide.*
Toronto: LexisNexis Canada.

**Relevance:** Includes secured transactions and payment flow analysis.
Useful for structural analysis of fund movements in payments contexts —
particularly where client payment flows involve secured assets or
escrow-like arrangements.

**Key coverage:** Commercial transactions, secured transactions, payment flows,
agency, trusts in commercial contexts.

**Retrieval:** Print / LexisNexis Canada (loose-leaf). Not available online.

## 4. Benjamin Geva — Payments Law Doctrine (Must-Read)

Benjamin Geva (Osgoode Hall / York University) is the leading academic authority
on payment systems law in Canada and comparatively. His work is foundational
to understanding payment finality, risk allocation, and the legal nature of
funds transfers.

### 4.1 Monographs

| Work | Citation | Key Topics |
|------|----------|------------|
| Bank Collections and Payment Transactions | Geva, B. (2001). *Bank Collections and Payment Transactions: A Comparative Analysis.* Oxford: Oxford University Press. | Funds transfers, intermediary bank roles, allocation of risk across parties in multi-bank payment chains |
| The Payment Order of Antiquity and the Middle Ages | Geva, B. (2011). *The Payment Order of Antiquity and the Middle Ages: A Legal History.* Oxford: Hart Publishing. | Historical analysis of payment obligations and instruments; useful for doctrinal grounding on the nature of payment |

### 4.2 Key Articles

| Citation | Journal | Key Argument | Relevance |
|----------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Geva, B. (1986). "Payment Finality and Discharge in Funds Transfers." *Canadian Business Law Journal,* 10 C.B.L.J. 435. | Can. Bus. L.J. | When payment is legally complete; finality doctrine | Payment risk analysis; when client obligations are discharged |
| Geva, B. (1990). "Risk Allocation in Funds Transfers." *Canadian Business Law Journal,* 16 C.B.L.J. 1. | Can. Bus. L.J. | Who bears loss in failed or interrupted payments | Core doctrine for payment failure and intermediary liability |
| Geva, B. (1992). "Credit Transfers and the Allocation of Risk." *Banking Law Journal,* 109 Banking L.J. 63. | Banking L.J. | Comparative + doctrinal; frequently cited in payment liability discussions | Particularly relevant for cross-border and credit transfer structures |
| Geva, B. (2003). "Discharge and Payment Finality in Funds Transfers." In Payment Systems and Other Financial Transactions (various edited volumes). | Edited volume | Expands earlier finality work; links doctrine to system design | Updated treatment; links to clearing system architecture |
| Geva, B. (2013). "Virtual Currencies and Block Chains: Legal Context and Implications for Financial Crime, Money Laundering and Tax Evasion." | SSRN / edited volume | One of few Geva pieces touching crypto/modern systems | Useful for crypto payments + AML intersection; bridges doctrinal foundation to modern context |

**Retrieval:** Monographs — print via academic library or online via Oxford
Scholarship Online / Hart Digital. Articles — WestlawNext Canada or HeinOnline.

## 5. Payments Canada — Infrastructure Publications

### 5.1 Payment Clearing and Settlement Systems in Canada

**Citation:** Payments Canada. (various publications). *Payment Clearing and
Settlement Systems in Canada.* Ottawa: Payments Canada.

**Relevance:** Semi-official explanatory material on Canada's payment
infrastructure. Key for understanding Lynx (high-value), AFT (automated funds
transfer/batch), and the clearing and settlement framework within which PSPs
operate. Directly relevant to RPAA operational requirements and payment rail
access matters.

**Key coverage:** LVTS / Lynx (high-value payments), AFT/batch clearing,
clearing frameworks, payment system rules, Payments Canada membership,
settlement finality.

**Retrieval:** https://www.payments.ca (publications section). Multiple reports
across years; confirm current editions.

### 5.2 Real-Time Rail (RTR) Modernization

**Citation:** Payments Canada. (ongoing). *Real-Time Rail Program.*
Ottawa: Payments Canada.

**URL:** https://www.payments.ca/systems-services/payment-systems/real-time-rail-payment-system

**Key facts:**
- RTR is Canada's forthcoming 24/7/365 real-time clearing and settlement system
- IBM Canada and CGI are delivery partners; Interac participating
- Technical build completed Q3 2025; industry testing commenced 2025–2026
- Payments Canada membership expansion (consultation February 2025) opens RTR
  access to non-bank PSPs — significant implication for RPAA-registered firms
- Lynx replaced LVTS in 2021 as Canada's high-value payment system (IBM Canada
  was lead technology partner)

**Relevance to LL:** RTR membership access is a critical issue for PSP clients.
Registration under RPAA is a prerequisite or facilitator of Payments Canada
membership. Clients seeking rail access need to understand this progression.
The February 2025 Payments Canada consultation on membership expansion is
directly relevant to the BANK_ONBOARD and ONGOING_PAYMENTS_COUNSEL solutions.

**Retrieval:** Retrieved (via search metadata). Direct access to full reports
pending — portal returned 403 on initial access.

## 6. International Monetary Law

### 6.1 Mann on the Legal Aspect of Money

**Citation:** Proctor, C. (latest ed.). *Mann on the Legal Aspect of Money*
(7th ed. or later). Oxford: Oxford University Press.

**Relevance:** The authoritative English-language treatise on the law of money
and monetary obligations. Originally by F. A. Mann; now maintained by Charles
Proctor. Essential for understanding the legal nature of money, monetary
obligations, discharge of payment obligations, foreign currency obligations,
and monetary sovereignty. Relevant to any matter touching payment finality,
currency denomination disputes, digital money characterization, and the
intersection of payment systems with private international law.

**Key coverage:** Legal concept of money (fiat, token, account), monetary
obligations and discharge, foreign currency obligations, monetary sovereignty,
sanctions and blocked payments, private international law of money, digital /
electronic money.

**Relevance to LL practice:** Foundational for characterizing stablecoins,
CBDCs, and e-money under existing monetary law doctrine. Directly relevant
to CARF scoping (what counts as a "crypto-asset" vs money) and to RPAA
(what qualifies as a "retail payment activity" involving electronic funds).
The treatise's treatment of electronic money and payment finality doctrine
connects to Geva's Canadian payment systems work.

**Retrieval:** Print / Oxford Scholarship Online. Not available free online.
Firm library access or OSO subscription required.

## 7. Academic and Policy Papers — Fintech Regulation

### 7.1 Regulatory Sandboxes and Smart Regulation

**Citation:** Zetzsche, D. A., Buckley, R. P., Arner, D. W., & Barberis, J.
(2017). "Regulating a Revolution: From Regulatory Sandboxes to Smart
Regulation." *Fordham Journal of Corporate & Financial Law,* 23(1).

**URL:** https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/jcfl/vol23/iss1/2/

**Abstract (retrieved):** Argues for "smart regulation" — an automated,
proportionate regulatory approach balancing financial stability and consumer
protection with innovation support. Traces the post-crisis regulatory pendulum;
proposes a regulatory spectrum from permissiveness through sandboxes to
digitally-native frameworks. Widely cited in Canadian fintech policy discussions.

**Relevance:** Background for understanding regulatory architecture of RPAA and
fintech-facing regulatory design. Useful context for client advisory on
regulatory strategy.

**Retrieval:** Retrieved (abstract level).

## 8. IMF Policy Papers — Digital Money and Stablecoins

### 7.1 The Rise of Digital Money

**Citation:** Adrian, T., & Mancini-Griffoli, T. (2019). *The Rise of Digital
Money.* IMF FinTech Notes No. 2019/001. Washington: IMF.

**URL:** https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/Fintech-Notes/Issues/2019/07/12/The-Rise-of-Digital-Money-47097

**Abstract (retrieved):** Examines how technology firms are disrupting
traditional banking and payment systems. Distinguishes e-money (electronically
stored, fiat-denominated) from unpegged cryptocurrencies. Identifies benefits
(efficiency, financial inclusion) and risks (monopoly formation, currency
destabilization, consumer protection gaps). Argues for regulatory frameworks
addressing non-bank e-money issuers.

**Relevance:** Foundational IMF taxonomy of digital money — useful for scoping
RPAA and CARF coverage questions. The e-money/stablecoin distinction maps
directly onto Canadian regulatory scope analysis.

**Retrieval:** Retrieved (abstract level).

---

### 7.2 Regulating the Crypto Ecosystem: The Case of Stablecoins

**Citation:** Bains, P., Ismail, A., Melo, F., & Sugimoto, N. (2022).
*Regulating the Crypto Ecosystem: The Case of Stablecoins and Arrangements.*
IMF FinTech Notes No. 2022/008. Washington: IMF.

**URL:** https://www.imf.org/en/publications/fintech-notes/issues/2022/09/26/regulating-the-crypto-ecosystem-the-case-of-stablecoins-and-arrangements-523724

**Abstract (retrieved):** Argues for comprehensive, consistent, risk-based
global stablecoin regulation. Recommends requirements covering entire ecosystem
and all key functions, with additional oversight for systemic arrangements.
Emphasizes domestic + international coordination. 47 pages.

**Relevance:** Directly relevant to CARF scoping (stablecoins on DLT are
in-scope reportable assets) and to future RPAA scope expansion to include
stablecoin payment functions (flagged in 2025 federal budget).

**Retrieval:** Retrieved (abstract level).
