# LL Finance Principles

## Purpose

Record the finance concepts that are relevant to Levine Law as a small professional-services business.

This is not a governance artifact. It is a finance lens: how the firm allocates scarce capital under uncertainty to increase owner value while preserving survival.

## Core Finance Question

How should Levine Law allocate scarce capital under uncertainty to increase owner value while preserving survival?

## Relevant Finance Concepts

### Time Value of Money

Cash now is worth more than cash later.

For Levine Law, this matters for accounts receivable, trust timing, delayed collections, buying a book of business, office/property decisions, and any decision where cash is exchanged now for uncertain future benefit.

### Risk-Adjusted Return

A dollar from a reliable retainer is not economically equivalent to a dollar from a slow-paying client.

The nominal revenue may be the same, but the finance value differs because of timing, uncertainty, collection risk, required working capital, and stress on liquidity.

This connects directly to the management accounting cash-risk categories in `../01_ACCOUNTING/LLP-001_ACCOUNTING/management_accounting/REVENUE_TIMING_SECURITY_TAXONOMY.md`:

| Accounting cash-risk category | Finance implication |
|---|---|
| Unsecured post-pay | Highest cash risk; revenue requires the firm to finance client work through WIP and AR. |
| Secured post-pay | Lower risk than unsecured AR if payment is credibly committed or reliably collectible. |
| Trust-funded earned fees | Lower cash risk, but operating-cash timing remains constrained by trust-transfer rules. |
| Recurring / subscription-like | Lowest cash risk if cash is available in advance, but professionally sensitive where fees are for future or unbilled legal services. |

Finance should therefore discount or haircut revenue streams for cash risk, even when accounting revenue is booked at the same nominal amount.

### Liquidity

The firm can be profitable and still fragile if cash arrives too late.

Liquidity is the ability to meet obligations when due, including rent, tax, payroll, software, credit cards, professional obligations, and owner needs.

### Working Capital

Working capital is the cash tied up funding clients, WIP, AR, taxes, staff, and fixed overhead before revenue is collected.

For Levine Law, weak WIP-to-invoice or invoice-to-cash conversion increases working capital strain.

### Capital Structure

Capital structure is the mix of shareholder funding, retained earnings, credit cards, debt facilities, and any other financing used to support the firm.

The same business can have very different risk depending on whether it is funded by collected cash, shareholder loans, credit cards, bank debt, or retained earnings.

### Cost of Capital

Every source of funding has a cost.

The cost may be explicit interest, owner stress, reduced flexibility, tax friction, risk concentration, personal guarantee exposure, dilution, or opportunity cost.

### Option Value

Cash, unused credit, flexible capacity, and clean banking relationships have value because they preserve choices.

For Levine Law, option value matters for opportunities such as recruiting a lawyer with a book, acquiring a book of business, acquiring property through LawCo, absorbing a downturn, or investing in a high-quality revenue channel.

### Operating Leverage

Fixed costs increase upside when revenue grows and increase danger when revenue is uneven.

Rent, employees, software, contractor commitments, credit obligations, and recurring overhead all increase the revenue consistency required to keep the firm stable.

### Asset-Liability Matching

Long-lived commitments should be funded with stable capital; short-term gaps should use short-term tools.

For Levine Law, this matters for leases, property, technology investments, credit cards, shareholder financing, and any decision that converts flexible cash into a fixed commitment.

### Portfolio Theory

The firm is a portfolio of matters, clients, fee earners, capital commitments, and cash claims.

The finance question is not only total revenue. It is the risk/return/liquidity profile of the portfolio: concentration, volatility, collectability, correlation, margin, timing, and resilience.

## Boundary

- Accounting records and management accounting categories describe the operating money system.
- Finance applies capital-allocation, risk, return, liquidity, and portfolio concepts to major firm decisions.
- Finance does not override accounting records or professional obligations.
