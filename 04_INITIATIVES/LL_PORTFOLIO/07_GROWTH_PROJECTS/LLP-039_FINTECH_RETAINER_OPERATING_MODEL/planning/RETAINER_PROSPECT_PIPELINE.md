## Active Prospects

### Albor

**Status:** Identified — blocked pending Andersen resolution  
**Added:** 2026-05-19  
**Source:** Andersen (market access relationship). Andersen principals are not
interested in the mandate. ML1 may not pursue Albor around Andersen — any
engagement must flow through the Andersen relationship, not directly.

**Product:** Stablecoin treasury infrastructure for banks. Global expansion strategy.

**Client base:** Banks (institutional), not retail or SME payments operators.
Albor provides treasury infrastructure that allows banks to hold, manage, and
settle stablecoin positions. This is institutional-grade infrastructure, not a
payments rail.

**Why this fits:**

Stream (anchor retainer) is fiat and stablecoin cross-border rails — the movement
layer. Albor is stablecoin treasury infrastructure for banks — the institutional
holding and management layer. These are structurally different products serving
entirely different client segments:

| | Stream | Albor |
|---|---|---|
| Product | Cross-border payment rails (fiat + stablecoin) | Stablecoin treasury infrastructure |
| Client | Fintech operators, B2B payments businesses | Banks (institutional) |
| Function | Move money across borders | Help banks hold and manage stablecoin positions |
| Regulatory surface | FINTRAC/MSB, RPAA/PSP | CARF, OSFI/Bank Act, MiCA, potentially FINTRAC |

No corridor competition. No client-base overlap. Expertise compounds: Stream and
Albor together cover the full spectrum of the fintech/payments/crypto regulatory
boutique thesis.

Regulatory exposure for stablecoin treasury infrastructure serving banks:

| Regime | Likely exposure |
|---|---|
| CARF | Reporting entity as a crypto-asset service provider (CASP) |
| FINTRAC / PCMLTFA | MSB registration (dealing in virtual currency) |
| Bank Act / OSFI | Significant — providing infrastructure to Canadian banks triggers bank vendor scrutiny; OSFI guidance on third-party risk applies |
| Stablecoin characterization | Bank Act deposit-taking constraints; payment stablecoin vs. security token distinction |
| Global expansion | Multi-jurisdiction: EU MiCA (stablecoin issuers heavily regulated); UK FCA (stablecoin regime in progress); US OCC/FinCEN guidance on bank stablecoin activity |
| RPAA | Lower likelihood — depends on whether treasury flows constitute retail payment activities |

The global expansion strategy means significant project work on top of retainer:
each new jurisdiction is a separate scoping exercise. ML1's role would likely be
Canada anchor plus coordination of external counsel in other markets — the Group
Head of Legal model in miniature.

This is the CARF client the practice is being built for. The Real-Time Rails vs.
Stablecoins thought leadership maps directly to Albor's market. fintechlawyer.ca
Cluster P8 (Stablecoin Regulatory Framework) positions ML1 as the right counsel
for exactly this conversation.

**Jurisdiction:** US-incorporated. Canadian regulatory exposure via global expansion
strategy. LL's role would be the Canadian regulatory layer.

**Conflict check:** No known conflict with Stream. Different product layer, different
client segment. LSO conflict check required before engagement.

**Andersen constraint:** LL may not pursue Albor around Andersen. Andersen is not
interested in the mandate and is not interested in facilitating a referral. The
prospect is on hold until the situation changes — either Andersen's position shifts
or the relationship structure changes in a way that opens a direct path.

Do not contact Albor directly.

**Open questions:**
- Which jurisdictions are in scope for Albor's global expansion (Canada confirmed?)?
- What is the approximate AUM or stablecoin volume under treasury management?
- What is the timeline and urgency of the Canadian regulatory question?
