# What Banks Ask During Fintech Onboarding (AML Focus)

Bank onboarding is not a document collection exercise. It is a structured attempt to understand how risk flows through a business.

For fintech companies, the questions asked during onboarding tend to converge around a small number of themes. The bank is not looking for broad descriptions. It is trying to determine whether the business can explain, in concrete terms, how money moves and how that movement is controlled.

## The Core Question Behind All Others

Most onboarding questions reduce to a single underlying issue.

How does this business move funds, and how is that activity monitored and controlled?

Everything else is a variation on that theme.

If the answer is clear, onboarding tends to progress. If it is not, the process slows quickly.

## Understanding Transaction Flows

Banks expect a precise explanation of how transactions occur.

This includes where funds enter the system, how they are routed, and how they reach their final destination. The focus is not on product features but on the mechanics of movement.

If the explanation relies on high-level descriptions or skips over intermediate steps, it becomes difficult for the bank to assess exposure.

## Control and Responsibility

Another consistent area of focus is control.

Banks want to understand who controls onboarding, who determines how transactions are structured, and who is responsible for monitoring activity.

In models involving multiple parties, this becomes more complex. The question is not just who participates in the flow, but who actually makes decisions within it.

Where responsibility is unclear, risk increases.

## Monitoring and Escalation

Banks also examine how suspicious activity is identified and handled.

This includes how monitoring systems operate, how alerts are reviewed, and how escalation decisions are made. The emphasis is on consistency.

If similar situations appear to be handled differently, or if escalation thresholds are not clearly defined, the system becomes difficult to rely on.

## Documentation and Explanation

Finally, banks assess whether the business can explain its decisions.

This is where documentation becomes central. The expectation is that the business can describe how specific transactions were assessed and why particular conclusions were reached.

If explanations are incomplete or inconsistent, the issue is not just documentation. It is whether the underlying process is coherent.

## The Practical Takeaway

Bank onboarding questions are not designed to gather information. They are designed to test whether the business understands and controls its own risk.

Where transaction flows, control points, and decision-making processes can be explained clearly, onboarding tends to move forward.

Where they cannot, the process becomes uncertain.
