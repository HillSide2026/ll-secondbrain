# What FINTRAC Reviews in Suspicious Transaction Reporting (MSBs in Canada)

Suspicious transaction reporting is not reviewed in isolation.

When FINTRAC conducts a compliance examination, it is assessing whether an AML program functions as a coherent system. Within that system, STR decision-making is one of the most closely examined areas.

The question is not whether reports were filed. It is whether the decisions behind those reports can be understood, supported, and applied consistently.

## How STR Decisions Are Evaluated

STR review typically occurs through transaction testing.

Specific transactions are selected and examined in detail. The objective is to understand how the business identified the activity, how it assessed it, and how it arrived at a decision.

This creates a direct line between individual transactions and the broader system.

If the handling of those transactions appears inconsistent or unsupported, the issue extends beyond the specific case.

## Identification of Suspicious Activity

The first point of review is how suspicious activity is identified.

This includes how monitoring systems are configured, how alerts are generated, and how those alerts are interpreted.

The focus is not on whether alerts exist. It is on whether the system is capable of identifying activity that should reasonably raise concern given how the business operates.

If relevant activity is not being identified, the issue is structural.

## Decision-Making and Threshold Application

Once activity is identified, the next question is how decisions are made.

FINTRAC assesses whether the standard of reasonable grounds to suspect is being applied appropriately. This does not require perfect judgment, but it does require consistency.

If similar fact patterns lead to different outcomes without a clear rationale, the issue is not the standard. It is how it is being applied.

Decision-making is evaluated across cases, not in isolation.

## Timing of Reporting

Timing is another point of scrutiny.

The expectation is that reports are filed once suspicion is formed, without unnecessary delay. Internal investigation is permitted, but it cannot replace decision-making.

If reporting appears consistently delayed, this may indicate that the internal threshold is set too high or that escalation processes are unclear.

## Documentation and Narrative

Documentation is central to the review.

For each transaction examined, the expectation is that the business can explain what was observed, how it was interpreted, and why a particular decision was reached.

This explanation must be supported by contemporaneous records.

If the reasoning cannot be reconstructed, the issue is not limited to documentation. It calls into question whether decisions are being made in a structured way.

## Consistency Across the System

The most important factor is consistency.

FINTRAC is not looking for isolated errors. It is assessing whether the system produces reliable outcomes.

If similar transactions are handled differently, or if reasoning varies across cases, the system becomes difficult to assess and difficult to rely on.

Consistency is what makes decisions defensible.

## What This Means in Practice

STR review is not a separate process. It is embedded within the broader evaluation of the AML program.

The examination tests whether the system:

- identifies relevant activity
- applies the correct threshold
- produces timely decisions
- documents those decisions clearly
- does so consistently across cases

Where these elements are aligned, the system holds.

Where they are not, issues surface quickly through transaction-level review.

## The Practical Takeaway

FINTRAC does not assess STRs as standalone filings.

It assesses the system that produces them.

The focus is on how suspicious activity is identified, how decisions are made, and whether those decisions can be explained and applied consistently.

The standard is not perfection. It is coherence.
