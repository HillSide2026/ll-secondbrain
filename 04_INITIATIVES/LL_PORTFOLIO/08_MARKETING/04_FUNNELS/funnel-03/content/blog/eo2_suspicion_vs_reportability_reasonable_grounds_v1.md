# Suspicion vs. Reportability: Understanding the "Reasonable Grounds" Standard

The standard for filing a suspicious transaction report in Canada is based on reasonable grounds to suspect.

This is not a high threshold. It does not require proof, confirmation, or certainty. It requires a basis for concern that is grounded in observable facts and reasonable interpretation.

The challenge is not understanding the standard. It is applying it consistently.

## Suspicion Is Not Certainty

A common mistake is treating suspicion as something that must be verified before reporting.

That is not the standard. The obligation arises before certainty is reached.

If reporting is delayed until activity is fully explained or confirmed, the threshold has already been exceeded.

## What "Reasonable Grounds" Means in Practice

Reasonable grounds are formed through a combination of observation and context.

A transaction may appear unusual on its own, but context determines whether that unusual nature creates suspicion. This includes customer history, transaction patterns, and how the activity fits within the business model.

The analysis is not mechanical. It requires interpretation.

## The Risk of Over-Interpretation

At the same time, not every unusual transaction creates reasonable grounds to suspect.

If every deviation leads to reporting, the system loses its ability to distinguish meaningful signals from normal variation.

The standard requires balance.

## Consistency Over Precision

The goal is not to apply the standard perfectly in every instance. It is to apply it consistently across similar situations.

If similar fact patterns produce different outcomes, the issue is not the standard. It is how it is being used.

## The Practical Takeaway

"Reasonable grounds to suspect" is a judgment standard.

It does not require certainty, but it does require a defensible basis for concern.

The effectiveness of an STR system depends on how consistently that judgment is applied.
