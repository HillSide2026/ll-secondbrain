# What Are Ongoing AML Obligations for MSBs in Canada

Registering as a money services business (MSB) in Canada is only the starting point. The substantive obligation is maintaining an operational anti-money laundering (AML) program that aligns with how the business actually functions.

Most regulatory risk does not arise at registration. It arises from how the business operates after registration.

## The Core AML Program Requirements

Canadian MSBs are required to implement and maintain a compliance program with five core elements:

- written policies and procedures
- a documented risk assessment
- a compliance officer
- ongoing training
- an independent review

These are not formalities. They are expected to reflect the actual transaction flows, customer types, and risk exposure of the business.

Generic or templated programs tend to fail under scrutiny because they do not map to real operations.

## Client Identification and KYC

MSBs must identify clients in accordance with FINTRAC requirements.

This includes:

- verifying identity at onboarding
- applying enhanced measures for higher-risk clients
- maintaining records of identification methods

The practical issue is alignment. KYC processes must match how users interact with the system.

Where onboarding is automated or embedded into a product, gaps often appear between technical flow and regulatory expectations.

## Ongoing Transaction Monitoring

Monitoring is not a one-time control. It is continuous.

MSBs are expected to:

- review transactions for unusual or suspicious activity
- apply risk-based monitoring thresholds
- adapt monitoring as business activity evolves

The difficulty is calibration. Overly rigid rules generate noise. Weak rules miss risk.

Effective monitoring reflects the specific patterns of the business, not generic assumptions.

## Suspicious Transaction Reporting (STRs)

MSBs must file suspicious transaction reports where there are reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction is related to money laundering or terrorist financing.

This is a judgment-based standard.

The most common failure is treating STRs as a high-certainty threshold. It is not. Suspicion, not proof, triggers the obligation.

Internal escalation processes are critical here. If decision-making is unclear, reporting becomes inconsistent.

## Recordkeeping Requirements

MSBs must maintain records of:

- client identification
- transactions
- STR-related analysis
- compliance program documentation

Recordkeeping is not just about retention. It is about reconstructability.

If a regulator or bank asks how a transaction was assessed, the expectation is that the reasoning can be traced clearly.

## Risk Assessment Must Reflect Reality

Every MSB must conduct and maintain a risk assessment.

This includes evaluating:

- products and services
- delivery channels
- geographic exposure
- client types

The key issue is specificity.

A risk assessment that does not reflect how funds actually move through the system is not useful. Regulators expect alignment between the written assessment and operational controls.

## Independent Review Requirement

MSBs must conduct periodic independent reviews of their AML program.

This is often misunderstood.

The purpose is not to confirm that documents exist. It is to assess whether:

- controls are functioning
- policies match operations
- risks are being managed effectively

Weak reviews tend to focus on documentation. Strong reviews focus on execution.

## The Practical Gap

The most common issue is not lack of effort. It is misalignment.

Businesses often:

- implement generic AML frameworks
- evolve product flows over time
- fail to update compliance controls accordingly

This creates a gap between what is written and what is happening.

That gap is where most regulatory and banking friction arises.

## The Practical Takeaway

Ongoing AML obligations for MSBs in Canada are operational, not theoretical.

The requirement is not just to have a compliance program, but to have one that accurately reflects how the business moves funds, manages risk, and makes decisions.

Registration is a discrete event. Compliance is continuous.
