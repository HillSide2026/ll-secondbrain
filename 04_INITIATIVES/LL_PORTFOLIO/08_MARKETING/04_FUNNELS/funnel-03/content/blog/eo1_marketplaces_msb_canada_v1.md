# Are Marketplaces Considered MSBs in Canada?

Marketplaces are not automatically classified as money services businesses (MSBs) in Canada.

Many are not.

But a marketplace that handles the flow of funds between buyers and sellers can fall within MSB scope depending on how payments are structured.

The key issue is not whether the business is "a marketplace." It is whether the platform is participating in the transmission of funds between parties.

## Why Marketplaces Trigger MSB Analysis

A typical marketplace does three things:

- connects buyers and sellers
- facilitates transactions
- coordinates payment and settlement

The first function is not regulated. The second and third can be.

If a marketplace only enables discovery and leaves payments entirely to third parties, it may fall outside MSB requirements.

If it plays an active role in receiving, holding, or distributing funds, the analysis changes.

## The Critical Question: Who Controls the Money Flow

The classification turns on control.

A marketplace may be considered within MSB scope if it:

- collects funds from buyers
- holds funds, even briefly
- distributes funds to sellers
- determines when and how payouts occur

This does not require the marketplace to hold funds in a traditional custodial sense. Control over routing or timing can be sufficient.

Where the platform sits in the middle of the transaction, it begins to resemble a money transmission model.

## Common Marketplace Payment Structures

Marketplace payment flows tend to fall into a few patterns.

In a direct payment model, the buyer pays the seller through a third-party processor, and the marketplace never touches the funds. This structure is less likely to trigger MSB classification, assuming the separation is real.

In a platform-led settlement model, the marketplace collects payment from the buyer and later distributes funds to the seller. This is the structure most likely to fall within MSB scope.

In a hybrid model, a payment partner processes transactions, but the marketplace controls onboarding, payout timing, or fund allocation. This creates ambiguity and often requires closer analysis.

The presence of a payment processor does not, on its own, determine the outcome.

## Split Payments and Allocation Logic

Many marketplaces implement split payment functionality, where funds are divided between multiple parties.

If the platform determines:

- how funds are split
- which parties receive payment
- when those payments are executed

…it is exercising control over the movement of funds.

This is often enough to bring the activity within MSB analysis, even if a third-party processor executes the transactions.

## Escrow-Like Behavior

Some marketplaces introduce delay or conditional release of funds.

This includes:

- holding funds until goods are delivered
- releasing funds based on milestones
- managing dispute-related payment holds

These features resemble escrow, even if not formally structured as such.

From a regulatory perspective, this increases the likelihood that the platform is participating in money transmission.

## Merchant (Seller) Onboarding

Seller onboarding is another signal of control.

Where a marketplace:

- approves sellers
- sets participation criteria
- manages seller accounts

…it is influencing who can receive funds through the platform.

This role becomes relevant when combined with control over payment flows.

## Where Misclassification Happens

The most common mistake is treating marketplaces as purely software platforms.

That is accurate from a product perspective. It is often incomplete from a regulatory perspective.

If the platform facilitates the movement of money between users, it may be performing a regulated function regardless of how it is described.

Another frequent issue is assuming that using a payment processor resolves the question. In practice, processors handle execution, not necessarily classification.

## Cross-Border Marketplaces

Marketplaces with cross-border activity introduce additional complexity.

If a platform:

- connects Canadian buyers or sellers
- enables payments into or out of Canada
- or supports international settlement

…it may still trigger Canadian MSB obligations even if based elsewhere.

This often surfaces during banking onboarding or expansion planning.

## When the Line Needs to Be Analyzed Closely

Some marketplace models are clearly outside MSB scope. Others are clearly within. Many fall in between.

Closer analysis is typically required where:

- funds pass through the platform, even indirectly
- payout timing is controlled by the marketplace
- multiple parties are paid from a single transaction
- third-party processors are involved but not determinative

In these cases, classification depends on how the transaction flow operates in practice.

## The Practical Takeaway

Marketplaces are not regulated because they are marketplaces.

They are assessed based on whether they participate in the movement of funds between buyers and sellers.

Where the platform is limited to enabling transactions without controlling payment flows, MSB registration may not be required.

Where the platform sits in the middle—collecting, allocating, or distributing funds—it is likely operating within the scope of Canada's AML framework.

The distinction is not conceptual. It is structural.
