# Building a Defensible STR Narrative

Every suspicious transaction decision produces a narrative, whether it is written clearly or not.

The question is whether that narrative can be understood, followed, and defended.

## What a Narrative Does

A narrative connects facts to a conclusion.

It explains what was observed, how it was interpreted, and why it led to suspicion.

Without that connection, the decision appears arbitrary.

## From Data to Interpretation

Transactions generate data. Monitoring systems generate alerts. Reviews generate observations.

A narrative turns those elements into a coherent explanation.

It identifies what matters within the data and explains how those elements relate to one another.

## Clarity Over Complexity

A defensible narrative is not necessarily a detailed one.

What matters is clarity.

The reasoning should be understandable without relying on unstated assumptions or implicit knowledge. It should be possible for another reviewer to follow the logic and reach the same conclusion.

## Consistency Across Cases

Narratives should follow a consistent structure across similar situations.

This does not mean that every case is identical. It means that the way reasoning is expressed remains stable.

Where narratives vary widely in structure or depth, it becomes difficult to demonstrate that decisions are made consistently.

## Narrative as Evidence

In a review or examination, the narrative becomes evidence.

It is used to assess whether the decision was reasonable given the information available at the time.

If the narrative is unclear or incomplete, the decision may appear unsupported, even if it was appropriate.

## The Practical Takeaway

A defensible STR narrative is one that clearly links observation, interpretation, and conclusion.

It does not rely on assumption or hindsight. It reflects the reasoning at the time the decision was made.

Where that reasoning is clear and consistent, the decision can be supported.

Where it is not, the system becomes difficult to defend.
