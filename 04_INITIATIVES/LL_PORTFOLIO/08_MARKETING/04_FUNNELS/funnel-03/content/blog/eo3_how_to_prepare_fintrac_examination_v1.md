# How to Prepare for a FINTRAC Examination (MSBs in Canada)

A FINTRAC examination is not a review of whether an AML program exists. It is an assessment of whether that program functions in a way that reflects the business as it actually operates.

Most deficiencies identified during examinations are not caused by missing policies or incomplete documentation. They arise from a more fundamental issue: misalignment between the transaction reality of the business and the structure of its compliance system.

Preparation, in that context, is not about assembling materials. It is about ensuring that the system produces outcomes that can be explained, supported, and repeated under scrutiny.

## What an Examination Is Really Testing

Examinations are often described in procedural terms, but the underlying question is straightforward. FINTRAC is assessing whether the business understands its own risk and whether its controls operate consistently against that risk.

This is why examinations tend to occur once a business has reached a certain level of activity or complexity. At that stage, the question is no longer whether a framework exists, but whether it has kept pace with how the business has evolved.

## Where Issues Actually Surface

The review typically becomes concrete through transaction testing. Individual transactions are selected and examined not in isolation, but as evidence of how the system functions.

At that point, several things become visible at once. It becomes clear whether monitoring systems are identifying the right signals, whether alerts are handled consistently, and whether decisions are documented in a way that can be reconstructed after the fact.

If outcomes appear inconsistent, or if reasoning cannot be traced, the issue is not limited to those transactions. It reflects how the system is operating more broadly.

## The Role of Suspicious Transaction Decisions

Suspicious transaction reporting is a central pressure point.

The standard is based on reasonable grounds to suspect, but in practice, businesses often apply thresholds that are closer to certainty. This leads to under-reporting and, more importantly, to decision-making that cannot be justified consistently.

During an examination, the focus is not only on whether reports were filed. It is on how the business arrived at its decisions, and whether similar situations are treated in similar ways.

Where that consistency is missing, the issue becomes structural.

## Risk Assessment as a Living Document

Risk assessments are often treated as static documents. In reality, they are expected to reflect the current state of the business.

If the product has introduced new payment flows, expanded into new jurisdictions, or changed its customer profile, those developments should be visible in the risk model.

When they are not, the gap becomes apparent during review. Controls appear disconnected from actual exposure, and the program begins to look outdated rather than incomplete.

## Documentation as Evidence, Not Formality

Documentation is often approached as a recordkeeping requirement. In an examination, it functions as evidence.

The expectation is that any decision—particularly those involving transaction monitoring or escalation—can be explained after the fact. That explanation must show what was observed, how it was assessed, and why a particular outcome was reached.

If that chain cannot be reconstructed clearly, the issue is not simply documentation. It is the integrity of the decision-making process itself.

## The Pattern Behind Most Deficiencies

Across examinations, the same pattern tends to emerge.

The business has evolved. Transaction flows have become more complex. New features have been introduced. At the same time, the compliance framework has remained anchored to an earlier version of the model.

This creates a situation where policies describe one system, monitoring is calibrated to another, and actual activity reflects a third.

The examination does not create this misalignment. It exposes it.

## The Practical Takeaway

Preparing for a FINTRAC examination is not a matter of strengthening documentation or refining individual controls in isolation.

It is about alignment.

The AML program must reflect how the business actually operates.
