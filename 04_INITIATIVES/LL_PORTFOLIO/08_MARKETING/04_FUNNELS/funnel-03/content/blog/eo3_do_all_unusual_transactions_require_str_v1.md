# Do All Unusual Transactions Require an STR?

Not all unusual transactions are suspicious. But all suspicious transactions are unusual in some way.

The distinction is important.

## Unusual vs Suspicious

An unusual transaction is one that deviates from expected behavior.

A suspicious transaction is one where that deviation creates reasonable grounds to suspect illicit activity.

The difference lies in interpretation.

## Explaining the Deviation

Many unusual transactions can be explained.

Changes in customer behavior, legitimate business activity, or one-off events may account for the deviation.

If the explanation is credible and consistent with available information, suspicion may not arise.

## When Unusual Becomes Suspicious

The threshold is crossed when the deviation cannot be reasonably reconciled with expected behavior.

This may occur when:

- explanations are inconsistent or incomplete
- patterns suggest intentional structuring
- activity does not align with known customer profile

At that point, suspicion becomes reasonable.

## Consistency in Decision-Making

The challenge is applying this distinction consistently.

If similar situations are treated differently, the issue is not the transaction itself. It is the decision framework.

Clear reasoning and documentation are essential.

## The Practical Takeaway

Unusual transactions do not automatically require reporting.

The obligation arises when the unusual nature of the activity leads to reasonable grounds for suspicion.

The distinction depends on context, judgment, and consistency in how decisions are made.
