# STRs Are About Suspicion — Not Proof

The obligation to file a suspicious transaction report is triggered by suspicion, not proof.

This distinction is simple in principle but difficult in practice.

Many systems operate as though suspicion must be confirmed before reporting. Additional information is gathered, transactions are reviewed repeatedly, and decisions are delayed in search of certainty.

That is not the standard.

Reasonable grounds to suspect is a lower threshold. It requires a basis for concern grounded in observable facts and reasonable interpretation. It does not require confirmation.

When reporting is delayed until activity is fully explained, the threshold has already been exceeded.

This creates a structural problem. The system becomes calibrated around certainty rather than suspicion. As a result, relevant activity may not be reported in a timely way.

At the same time, applying the standard too loosely can produce excessive reporting. Not every unusual transaction creates reasonable grounds to suspect.

The challenge is not choosing between suspicion and proof. It is applying the correct threshold consistently.

Suspicion requires judgment. That judgment must be grounded, repeatable, and aligned with how the business actually operates.
