# One Indicator Is Rarely Enough

Suspicion rarely arises from a single indicator.

Most transactions contain elements that, in isolation, can be explained. Size, frequency, or routing may appear unusual, but not necessarily suspicious on their own.

The significance emerges when indicators are considered together.

A series of smaller transactions may suggest structuring. Changes in behavior over time may indicate shifting intent. Interactions with certain counterparties may elevate concern when combined with other factors.

The analysis is cumulative.

Relying on a single indicator creates two risks. Relevant activity may be dismissed because no single element appears significant. Alternatively, activity may be escalated prematurely based on one factor without sufficient context.

A consistent framework evaluates how indicators interact.

Suspicion forms when the combination of factors cannot be reconciled with expected behavior in a credible way.

The strength of the signal comes from how the pieces fit together, not from any single element.
