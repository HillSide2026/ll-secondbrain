# What Happens After You File an STR in Canada

Filing a suspicious transaction report does not produce a visible outcome.

There is no confirmation that the report was useful. There is no indication of whether the suspicion was well-founded. In most cases, there is no response at all.

This is by design. The reporting regime is built to collect information, not to provide feedback.

For the reporting business, that creates a practical gap. The obligation is clear. What follows is not.

## No Feedback from FINTRAC

Once an STR is submitted, it enters FINTRAC's system without direct response to the reporting entity.

There is no acknowledgment beyond receipt. There is no evaluation shared. There is no signal as to whether the report aligns with broader intelligence.

This means that reporting decisions must stand on their own. They are not validated externally.

The absence of feedback is not a flaw in the system. It is a feature of how it operates.

## The Obligation Does Not End with Filing

Filing an STR does not resolve the underlying issue.

If the activity continues, the business is expected to continue assessing it. The initial report becomes part of an ongoing evaluation, not a conclusion.

This creates a continuing obligation to monitor, reassess, and determine whether further reporting is required.

The reporting event is a point in time. The risk is not.

## Internal Records Become the Only Explanation

After a report is filed, the internal record becomes the only account of how the decision was made.

The expectation is that the business can explain what triggered suspicion, how the activity was analyzed, and why reporting was the appropriate outcome.

That explanation must exist independently of any external confirmation.

If the reasoning is not clearly documented, it cannot be reconstructed later. At that point, the issue is not the report itself. It is whether the decision can be defended.

## The Client Relationship Is a Separate Decision

Filing an STR does not determine whether a client relationship continues.

That decision depends on how the business evaluates its own risk tolerance and control framework.

In some cases, activity can continue under closer monitoring. In others, the relationship may no longer be viable.

The reporting obligation and the commercial decision are related, but they are not the same.

## Future Scrutiny Happens Later

Even without immediate feedback, STRs can become relevant at a later stage.

This may occur during a regulatory review, an examination, or a broader investigation. At that point, past decisions are revisited with the benefit of additional context.

The quality of the original analysis and documentation becomes important then, not at the time of filing.

## The System Relies on Internal Consistency

Because there is no external validation, the system depends on internal consistency.

Similar fact patterns should lead to similar outcomes. Decisions should be made according to a framework that can be applied repeatedly.

If reporting appears inconsistent, the issue is not individual transactions. It is the reliability of the process.

## The Practical Takeaway

Filing an STR is not an endpoint. It is part of an ongoing obligation to assess risk and make defensible decisions.

The absence of feedback does not reduce that obligation. It reinforces it.

The question is not whether a report was correct in hindsight. It is whether the decision to file can be supported based on the information available at the time, and whether similar decisions would be made again under similar conditions.
