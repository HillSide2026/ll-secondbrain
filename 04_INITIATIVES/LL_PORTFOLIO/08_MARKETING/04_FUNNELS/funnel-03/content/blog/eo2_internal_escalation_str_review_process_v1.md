# Internal Escalation: Designing an STR Review Process

Suspicious transaction reporting depends less on individual judgment than on how that judgment is structured.

An escalation process is what turns isolated observations into consistent decisions. Without it, similar situations are handled differently, timelines vary, and outcomes become difficult to explain.

## Where Escalation Begins

Escalation starts when activity cannot be resolved at the initial review level.

At that point, the question is not simply whether the transaction is unusual. It is whether the available information is sufficient to determine whether suspicion exists.

If that determination cannot be made clearly, the matter moves upward.

## Structure Over Discretion

In many organizations, escalation is informal.

Decisions are passed along based on perceived complexity or uncertainty, rather than defined criteria. Over time, this creates variability. Some issues are escalated quickly, while others remain at lower levels longer than they should.

A structured process reduces that variability.

Escalation should be based on identifiable triggers, not individual discretion.

## Defining Escalation Thresholds

Clear thresholds are critical.

These thresholds do not need to capture every possible scenario, but they should define when activity requires additional review. This may be based on transaction patterns, inconsistencies in explanation, or indicators that cannot be resolved at the initial stage.

Without defined thresholds, escalation becomes inconsistent.

## Timing and Decision Flow

Escalation also affects timing.

If the process is unclear, decisions slow down. Reviews are repeated, information is revisited, and responsibility becomes diffused.

A defined structure ensures that once activity meets escalation criteria, it moves efficiently toward a decision.

## Consistency Across Reviewers

A structured escalation process supports consistency across different reviewers.

If the same triggers lead to escalation regardless of who identifies them, the system becomes more predictable. Decisions are then based on shared criteria rather than individual interpretation.

## The Practical Takeaway

An STR review process is not just about identifying suspicious activity. It is about ensuring that decisions are made in a consistent and timely way.

Escalation is the mechanism that connects detection to decision.

Where that mechanism is unclear, outcomes become inconsistent. Where it is structured, the system becomes more reliable.
