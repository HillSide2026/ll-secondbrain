# The Cost of Inconsistent STR Decisions

Inconsistent STR decisions are not always visible immediately.

A single decision may appear reasonable in isolation. The problem emerges over time, as similar situations produce different outcomes without a clear explanation.

## Variability as a Signal

Inconsistency is often the first indication that the system is not functioning as intended.

If similar transactions are treated differently, the issue is not the transactions themselves. It is how they are being evaluated.

This variability makes it difficult to define what the system is actually doing.

## Impact on Defensibility

When decisions vary, they become harder to defend.

A regulator or bank does not assess decisions individually. It assesses whether the system produces consistent outcomes.

If similar cases lead to different conclusions, the question becomes which decision reflects the correct application of the standard.

## Erosion of Internal Confidence

Inconsistency also affects internal processes.

Reviewers may become uncertain about how to apply thresholds. Escalation may increase, not because activity is more complex, but because decision-making is less predictable.

Over time, this reduces confidence in the system.

## Link to Documentation and Escalation

Inconsistent decisions are often linked to weak documentation and unclear escalation.

If reasoning is not recorded clearly, it is difficult to apply it in future cases. If escalation thresholds are not defined, similar issues may be handled at different levels.

These factors reinforce variability.

## The Practical Takeaway

The cost of inconsistent STR decisions is not limited to individual outcomes.

It affects the reliability of the entire system.

Consistency is what makes decisions defensible. Without it, the system becomes difficult to explain, difficult to apply, and difficult to trust.
