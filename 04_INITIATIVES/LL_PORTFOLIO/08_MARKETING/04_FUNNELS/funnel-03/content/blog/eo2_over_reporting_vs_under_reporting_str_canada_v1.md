# Over-Reporting vs. Under-Reporting: Calibrating STR Decisions in Canadian MSBs

Suspicious transaction reporting is often framed as a binary obligation. In practice, it is a calibration problem.

The system must identify when suspicion reaches a level that requires reporting. If that threshold is set too high, relevant activity is not reported. If it is set too low, reporting becomes excessive and loses meaning.

Both outcomes reflect the same issue. The system is not calibrated to the business.

## The Risk of Under-Reporting

Under-reporting typically arises when suspicion is treated as something that must be confirmed.

Internal processes may require additional investigation, higher levels of approval, or stronger evidence before a report is filed. Over time, this raises the effective threshold above what the standard requires.

The result is that activity meeting the "reasonable grounds to suspect" threshold is not reported in a timely way, or at all.

This is rarely intentional. It is a consequence of caution being applied in the wrong place.

## The Risk of Over-Reporting

Over-reporting tends to occur when the system lacks confidence in its own decision-making.

If escalation criteria are unclear or inconsistent, the default response may be to report rather than assess. This shifts the burden of decision-making outward, but it does not resolve the underlying issue.

Excessive reporting can make it more difficult to distinguish meaningful activity from normal variation. It also indicates that the system is not effectively filtering risk.

## Calibration Depends on Context

There is no universal threshold that applies across all businesses.

Calibration depends on how the business operates, how transactions behave, and what types of activity are expected within that model.

A system that is appropriate for one set of transaction patterns may be ineffective for another.

This is why calibration cannot be static. It must reflect the current state of the business.

## Consistency as the Indicator

The clearest sign of proper calibration is consistency.

Similar fact patterns should lead to similar outcomes. Decisions should not depend on who reviews the transaction or how it is escalated.

Where outcomes vary without a clear rationale, calibration is likely off.

## The Practical Takeaway

STR decision-making is not about choosing between reporting and not reporting. It is about applying a threshold that reflects the standard and the business.

Under-reporting and over-reporting are not opposite problems. They are different expressions of the same issue.

The objective is not precision. It is consistency aligned with the level of risk the business actually presents.
