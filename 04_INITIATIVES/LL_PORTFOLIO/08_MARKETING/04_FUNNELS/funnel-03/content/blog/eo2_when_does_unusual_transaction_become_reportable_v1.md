# When Does an Unusual Transaction Become Reportable?

Unusual transactions are common. Reportable transactions are not.

The distinction between the two is where most STR decisions are made.

## Starting with Deviation

An unusual transaction is one that does not align with expected behavior.

This may involve size, frequency, structure, or routing. The deviation itself is not the issue. It is the starting point for analysis.

## The Role of Explanation

The next step is whether the deviation can be explained.

If there is a credible and consistent explanation that aligns with available information, the activity may remain unusual but not suspicious.

If the explanation is incomplete, inconsistent, or implausible, the analysis shifts.

## From Deviation to Suspicion

An unusual transaction becomes reportable when the deviation cannot be reconciled with expected behavior in a way that removes reasonable concern.

At that point, suspicion is formed.

The reporting obligation follows from that moment, not from further investigation.

## Patterns Matter

Single transactions may not provide enough context to form suspicion.

Patterns of behavior, however, can change the analysis. Repeated deviations, changes in activity over time, or structured transactions may elevate the level of concern.

The assessment is cumulative.

## Consistency in Application

The challenge is not identifying unusual activity. It is deciding when that activity crosses the threshold into suspicion.

This requires a consistent framework.

Without it, similar transactions may be treated differently, and the system becomes unreliable.

## The Practical Takeaway

An unusual transaction becomes reportable when it creates reasonable grounds to suspect that something is wrong.

The distinction depends on context, explanation, and consistency in how those factors are evaluated.
