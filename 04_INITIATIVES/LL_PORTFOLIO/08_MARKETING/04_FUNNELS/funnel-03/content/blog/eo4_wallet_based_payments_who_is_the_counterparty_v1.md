# Wallet-Based Payments: Who Is the Counterparty?

In traditional payment systems, counterparties are generally identifiable.

Accounts are linked to individuals or entities. Transactions occur between known participants.

Wallet-based systems disrupt this assumption.

## Addresses and Identity

A wallet address represents a point in a transaction flow.

It does not necessarily represent a person, a business, or even a single user. Multiple individuals may control a single address, and a single individual may control many.

This creates ambiguity in identifying counterparties.

## The Problem of Attribution

AML analysis depends on understanding who is involved in a transaction.

In wallet-based systems, attribution is not always straightforward. Additional information may be required to determine whether an address is associated with a known entity, an exchange, or an unknown party.

Without that context, interpretation becomes uncertain.

## Interaction with Known Entities

Some transactions involve interaction with identifiable platforms, such as exchanges or payment providers.

These interactions can provide anchor points for analysis. They offer moments where identity and activity align more clearly.

Between those points, the picture may be less certain.

## Implications for Suspicion

Uncertainty in attribution does not eliminate suspicion. It changes how suspicion is formed.

Patterns of behavior, interaction with known counterparties, and transaction history become more important than any single data point.

The analysis shifts from identity-based to behavior-based.

## The Practical Takeaway

Wallet-based systems require a different approach to counterparty analysis.

Identity may not be directly observable, but behavior remains visible.

Suspicion must be formed based on how activity unfolds, rather than on who is definitively known to be involved.
