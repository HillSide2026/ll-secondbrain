# Does a Crypto or Stablecoin Business Count as an MSB in Canada?

A crypto or stablecoin business may be required to register as a money services business (MSB) in Canada if it performs certain activities involving virtual currency.

The key point is structural: Canadian AML rules apply to what the business does with virtual currency, not how the product is described.

In most cases, businesses that exchange, transfer, or custody crypto assets fall within MSB scope. Stablecoin models are not treated differently simply because of the asset type.

## How Canada Regulates Virtual Currency Businesses

Under FINTRAC's framework, virtual currency businesses are explicitly included within the definition of an MSB.

This applies where a business is engaged in:

- exchanging virtual currency for fiat or other virtual currency
- transferring virtual currency between parties
- providing custodial services over virtual currency

The regulatory approach is functional. It focuses on whether the business is participating in the movement or control of value.

There is no separate category for "crypto companies" or "stablecoin platforms." The same MSB framework applies.

## Exchange Activity: The Clearest Trigger

Businesses that operate exchanges are almost always within scope.

This includes platforms that:

- convert fiat to crypto
- convert crypto to crypto
- facilitate trading between users

Even where the platform does not take proprietary positions, the act of enabling exchange transactions is sufficient.

From a regulatory perspective, this is a direct form of value transfer.

## Transfer Activity: Often Overlooked

Some businesses do not operate exchanges but still facilitate transfers.

This includes:

- payment applications using crypto rails
- platforms that move assets between users
- systems that route transactions across wallets

Where a business is involved in transmitting virtual currency from one party to another, it may be performing an MSB activity even without conversion.

This is a common source of misclassification, particularly in payment-oriented crypto products.

## Custody as a Decisive Factor

Custody is one of the clearest indicators of MSB status.

Where a business:

- holds private keys
- controls access to user funds
- manages wallets on behalf of users

…it is typically considered to be providing custodial services.

Custodial wallet providers are generally within MSB scope because they exercise control over client assets.

## Non-Custodial Models: Not Automatically Out of Scope

Non-custodial models are often assumed to fall outside MSB requirements. That is sometimes correct, but not always.

If a system:

- facilitates transfers
- orchestrates transactions
- or plays an active role in routing assets

…the absence of custody may not be determinative.

Hybrid models are particularly difficult. Many products described as non-custodial still retain elements of control that are relevant to the analysis.

## Stablecoins Do Not Change the Framework

Stablecoin businesses often assume they are treated differently because the asset is pegged or designed for stability.

In Canada, that distinction is not central to AML classification.

The relevant questions remain:

- Is the business issuing or redeeming the asset?
- Is it facilitating transfers between users?
- Does it control wallets or access to funds?

A stablecoin used for payments can trigger the same MSB analysis as any other form of virtual currency.

## Issuance vs Transfer vs Infrastructure

Stablecoin models introduce structural variation, but not regulatory exemption.

An issuer that creates and redeems tokens may fall within scope depending on how those tokens circulate and are controlled.

A platform that enables users to send stablecoins is more clearly engaged in transfer activity.

An infrastructure provider that only supplies software tools, without participating in transactions, may fall outside the framework—but only if that separation is real in practice.

The classification depends on where the business sits in the transaction lifecycle.

## Cross-Border Activity and Canadian Exposure

MSB obligations can apply even where a crypto or stablecoin business is based outside Canada.

If the business:

- serves Canadian users
- enables access from Canada
- or facilitates transactions involving Canadian participants

…it may be considered to be operating in Canada for AML purposes.

This issue often arises during expansion or banking onboarding, rather than at initial launch.

## Where the Analysis Becomes Necessary

Straightforward exchange or custodial models are usually easy to classify.

More complex structures require closer analysis, particularly where:

- custody is split across multiple parties
- transaction flows are automated or programmable
- stablecoin issuance is combined with payment functionality
- third-party infrastructure providers are involved

In these cases, the classification turns on how the system actually operates, not how it is described.

## The Practical Framing

The useful question is not whether a business is "a crypto company" or "a stablecoin platform."

It is whether, when mapped in detail, the business:

- exchanges value
- transfers value
- or controls access to value

If any of those functions are present in a meaningful way, MSB registration in Canada is likely part of the regulatory analysis.

That conclusion tends to follow from the structure itself, not the label attached to it.
