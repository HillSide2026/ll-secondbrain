# Common AML Program Failures in Canadian MSBs

Money services businesses in Canada require an AML program. And the program needs to stand the test of time.

Even with the best of intentions, problems may emerge as the business grows.

As the business evolves, the compliance framework often does not. What starts well enough gradually becomes disconnected from how the business actually operates. These failures are not isolated issues. They are symptoms of a deeper structural misalignment.

## Misalignment Between Product and Compliance

The most consistent failure is a disconnect between how funds move through the business and how those flows are described and monitored within the AML program.

Products change. New payment flows are introduced. Customer behavior shifts. In many cases, the compliance framework continues to reflect an earlier version of the business.

The result is a system that appears complete but no longer corresponds to reality. Monitoring rules begin to miss relevant activity. Risk assessments become outdated. Policies describe processes that no longer exist in practice.

This is not immediately visible internally. It becomes visible under scrutiny.

## Weak Transaction Monitoring

Transaction monitoring systems often fail not because they are absent, but because they are poorly calibrated.

Over time, rules that were initially appropriate become either too narrow or too broad. Some activity is no longer identified, while other activity generates alerts that do not correspond to meaningful risk.

As alert volumes increase, review processes tend to become faster and less consistent. The system continues to operate, but its outputs become less reliable.

The issue is not the presence of monitoring. It is whether the system reflects the current behavior of the business.

## Inconsistent Suspicious Transaction Decisions

Suspicious transaction decision-making frequently lacks consistency.

Similar fact patterns may lead to different outcomes depending on who reviews the activity or how it is escalated. Internal thresholds may be unclear, and guidance may be applied unevenly.

The result is not only potential under-reporting or over-reporting. It is a lack of defensibility.

When decisions cannot be explained consistently across similar cases, the issue is not individual judgment. It is the integrity of the system.

## Inadequate Risk Assessment

Risk assessments are often treated as static documents rather than as reflections of the business in its current state.

As operations expand, particularly across new payment flows or jurisdictions, those changes are not always incorporated into the risk model. The assessment remains high-level and disconnected from actual transaction behavior.

At that point, controls derived from the assessment no longer align with real exposure. The framework exists, but it no longer functions as intended.

## Documentation Gaps

Documentation failures are rarely about the absence of records. They are about the absence of clear reasoning.

Transaction reviews may be recorded, but the rationale behind decisions is often incomplete or inconsistent. Different reviewers document similar situations in different ways, making it difficult to trace how conclusions were reached.

When documentation cannot support a decision, the issue is not administrative. It calls into question whether the decision-making process itself is reliable.

## Governance Weaknesses

In some organizations, compliance functions exist but do not operate with sufficient authority or integration.

Oversight may be limited, escalation may be delayed, and compliance may be treated as a parallel function rather than one embedded in operations.

These weaknesses rarely appear in isolation. They tend to amplify other issues, particularly where monitoring, reporting, and documentation are already misaligned.

## The Pattern Behind These Failures

Across these areas, the pattern is consistent.

The business changes. The compliance system does not.

Over time, the gap between the two becomes visible. It is usually identified not through internal review, but through external scrutiny — by a bank, a partner, or a regulator.

## The Practical Takeaway

AML program failures in Canadian MSBs are not typically caused by lack of awareness or effort.

They arise from misalignment between product, operations, and compliance controls.

Addressing these failures is not a matter of adding more documentation or refining isolated processes. It requires ensuring that the compliance system reflects how the business actually functions.

Where that alignment exists, the program holds.

Where it does not, the system breaks under scrutiny.
