# Do You Need a Remittance License in Canada? (MSB Rules Explained)

Canada does not have a separate "remittance license."

A business that offers remittance or money transfer services is generally required to register as a money services business (MSB) with FINTRAC.

In other words, remittance activity is not regulated as its own category. It is captured within the broader MSB framework.

The practical question is not whether a business is "a remittance company," but whether its activities qualify as money transmission under Canadian AML rules.

## What Counts as Remittance in Canada

Remittance is not formally defined as a standalone legal category. It is understood functionally as:

- sending money from one person to another
- typically across borders, but not necessarily
- often involving conversion or settlement across different systems

From a regulatory perspective, this maps directly onto money transmission.

If a business accepts funds for the purpose of transferring them to another party, it is likely engaging in MSB activity.

## Domestic vs Cross-Border Transfers

Many businesses assume that only cross-border transfers trigger remittance obligations.

That is incorrect.

Canadian AML rules apply to:

- international remittances
- domestic money transfers

The regulatory trigger is the movement of funds, not the geography.

Cross-border activity can introduce additional complexity, but it is not required for MSB classification.

## Common Remittance Business Models

Remittance today is rarely a standalone product. It is embedded into broader systems.

Common structures include:

- consumer transfer apps
- B2B cross-border payment platforms
- payroll or contractor payout systems
- marketplace settlement flows

These models often do not identify themselves as remittance businesses. That does not change the analysis.

If funds are being transmitted between parties, the underlying activity is still assessed as money transmission.

## Where Founders Misclassify Remittance Activity

The most common issue is treating remittance as a branding decision rather than a functional one.

A business may describe itself as:

- a payments platform
- a treasury tool
- an infrastructure provider

…but still be performing remittance activity if it facilitates the movement of funds.

Another common assumption is that using a banking partner or payment processor removes regulatory obligations.

In practice, that depends on whether the business retains control over how funds are received, routed, and delivered.

If that control exists, MSB analysis remains relevant.

## Remittance and Crypto-Based Transfers

Crypto and stablecoin-based transfer models are often positioned as alternatives to traditional remittance.

From a Canadian regulatory perspective, the mechanism does not eliminate the classification.

If a business:

- enables users to send value to another party
- facilitates conversion between fiat and crypto
- or controls wallet infrastructure

…it may still be engaged in activities that fall within MSB scope.

The use of blockchain does not remove the underlying function of money transmission.

## What Registration Actually Requires

Businesses engaged in remittance activity must register as MSBs and implement an AML compliance program.

This includes:

- client identification procedures
- transaction monitoring
- suspicious transaction reporting
- recordkeeping requirements

Registration is the entry point, not the full obligation.

For many businesses, the operational impact of compliance is more significant than the registration process itself.

## Banking and Partner Expectations

Remittance businesses are subject to heightened scrutiny from financial institutions.

Banks and payment partners typically require:

- confirmation of MSB registration
- a clear explanation of transaction flows
- evidence of an operational AML program

Where remittance activity is present but not clearly addressed, onboarding delays are common.

This is often where classification issues surface in practice.

## Cross-Border Businesses Serving Canada

A business does not need to be based in Canada to trigger MSB obligations.

If remittance services are offered to:

- Canadian residents
- users located in Canada
- or transactions involving Canadian endpoints

registration requirements may still apply.

This is particularly relevant for international fintechs expanding into the Canadian market.

## The Structural Question

The relevant question is not whether a business calls itself a remittance provider.

It is whether, when mapped step-by-step, the business is:

- receiving funds from one party
- transmitting those funds to another
- or facilitating that transfer in a meaningful way

If that function exists, the activity is likely captured within Canada's MSB regime.

Remittance is simply the market term for it.
