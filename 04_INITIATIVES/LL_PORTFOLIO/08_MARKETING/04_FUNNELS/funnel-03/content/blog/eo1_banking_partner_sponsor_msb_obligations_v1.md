# Does Using a Banking Partner or Sponsor Remove MSB Obligations in Canada?

Using a banking partner or sponsor does not automatically remove the need to register as a money services business (MSB) in Canada.

In some structures, it can. In many, it does not.

The determining factor is not the presence of a bank. It is whether the business has actually outsourced the regulated activity, or whether it continues to perform it in practice.

## Why This Assumption Is So Common

Many fintech models are built on top of banking or payment partners.

The logic seems straightforward:

- the bank is regulated
- the partner handles payments
- therefore, the fintech is not subject to MSB requirements

This reasoning is attractive and often wrong.

Canadian AML rules do not assign obligations based on who is "involved" in a transaction. They apply based on who is functionally performing the activity.

If a business is still participating in the movement of funds, the presence of a partner does not resolve the issue.

## What Sponsorship Actually Does

A banking partner or sponsor typically provides:

- access to payment rails
- regulated infrastructure
- execution of transactions

What it does not automatically do is assume responsibility for:

- how users or merchants are onboarded
- how transactions are structured or routed
- how funds are allocated or distributed

Those elements often remain with the fintech.

And those are precisely the elements that drive MSB analysis.

## Delegation vs Retention of Control

The distinction comes down to whether regulated functions are fully delegated or partially retained.

In a fully delegated model, the partner:

- onboards customers directly
- controls transaction execution
- manages settlement
- assumes compliance responsibility

In that structure, the fintech may be providing technology or interface only.

In a partially delegated model, the fintech:

- controls onboarding criteria
- determines transaction logic
- influences settlement timing or flow

Even if a bank executes the payment, the fintech may still be performing core aspects of money transmission.

That is where MSB obligations can remain.

## The Control Test Still Applies

As with other classification questions, control is central.

If a business determines:

- who can access the system
- how funds move between participants
- when and how payments are completed

…it is exercising control over the flow of funds.

That control does not disappear because a bank is involved.

The regulatory analysis follows the function, not the partnership structure.

## Where Misclassification Typically Happens

The most common mistake is treating sponsorship as a blanket exemption.

This often comes from:

- relying on partner marketing or positioning
- assuming compliance is fully "handled" externally
- focusing on contractual allocation rather than operational reality

In practice, agreements may assign responsibilities, but regulators will assess how the system actually works.

If the fintech is integral to the movement of funds, it may still fall within MSB scope.

## Embedded Finance and Platform Models

This issue is particularly common in embedded finance.

Platforms that:

- integrate payments into software
- enable merchant or contractor payouts
- manage multi-party transaction flows

often rely on sponsor banks or processors.

Even so, the platform may:

- control onboarding
- define transaction rules
- manage settlement logic

In these cases, the presence of a sponsor does not eliminate regulatory exposure. It complicates it.

## Cross-Border Structures

Cross-border models add another layer.

A fintech may rely on:

- a foreign bank
- an international payment partner
- a multi-jurisdictional structure

None of these automatically remove Canadian obligations if the business serves Canadian users or facilitates transactions involving Canada.

This is frequently identified during expansion or banking onboarding.

## What Banks Actually Expect

Banks and sponsors are aware of this distinction.

They typically require clarity on:

- whether the fintech is itself performing regulated activity
- how responsibilities are divided in practice
- whether MSB registration is required

If those questions are not answered clearly, onboarding can stall—even where a sponsorship model is in place.

## When the Structure Needs to Be Re-Examined

A deeper analysis is usually required where:

- the fintech controls key elements of the payment flow
- responsibilities are split across multiple parties
- the product has evolved beyond its original structure
- external stakeholders require a defensible classification

In these situations, the issue is not just whether a partner exists, but whether the current model aligns with how Canadian rules allocate responsibility.

## The Practical Takeaway

A banking partner or sponsor can provide infrastructure and, in some cases, absorb regulatory obligations.

It does not automatically do so.

If a fintech continues to control onboarding, transaction logic, or settlement, it may still be performing activities that fall within Canada's MSB framework.

The presence of a partner is relevant. It is not determinative.

The classification depends on who is actually doing the work inside the payment flow.
