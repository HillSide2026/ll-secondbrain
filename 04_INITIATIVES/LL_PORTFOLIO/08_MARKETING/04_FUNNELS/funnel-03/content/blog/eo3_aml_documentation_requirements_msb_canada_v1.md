# AML Documentation Requirements for MSBs in Canada

AML documentation is often treated as a compliance formality. In practice, it is the primary way a business demonstrates that its decisions are coherent and defensible.

The purpose of documentation is not to record activity. It is to explain it.

## Documentation as Evidence

Every compliance decision creates a record.

When a transaction is reviewed, the expectation is that the business can explain:

- what was observed
- how it was assessed
- why a particular conclusion was reached

This explanation must be supported by documentation.

If the reasoning cannot be reconstructed, the decision cannot be defended.

## Where Documentation Fails

Documentation failures rarely involve complete absence of records.

More often, the issue is that records exist but do not clearly explain the decision.

Notes may be brief or inconsistent. Reasoning may be implied rather than stated. Different reviewers may document similar situations in different ways.

This creates ambiguity.

When documentation is unclear, it becomes difficult to determine whether decisions were made consistently or appropriately.

## Inconsistent Standards

Many organizations do not define clear standards for documentation.

As a result, documentation quality varies depending on:

- who reviewed the transaction
- how complex the situation appeared
- how much time was available

This variability becomes a problem under scrutiny.

If documentation is not consistent, it is difficult to demonstrate that the system itself is consistent.

## Link to Monitoring and Reporting

Documentation is closely tied to both monitoring and reporting.

Monitoring generates alerts. Documentation explains how those alerts were handled.

Reporting decisions depend on whether activity meets the threshold for suspicion. Documentation must explain how that threshold was assessed.

Without clear documentation, both processes lose transparency.

## Recordkeeping Expectations

Regulators and financial institutions expect that records allow them to follow the full lifecycle of a decision.

This includes:

- the initial trigger
- the analysis performed
- the conclusion reached

The expectation is not just that records exist, but that they are sufficient to reconstruct the reasoning.

## When Documentation Becomes Critical

Documentation becomes most important when decisions are questioned.

This typically occurs during:

- regulatory reviews
- bank onboarding or reassessment
- internal investigations

At that point, the quality of documentation determines whether the business can explain its actions.

## The Practical Takeaway

AML documentation is not an administrative task. It is a core component of the compliance system.

Its function is to make decisions transparent, consistent, and defensible.

Where documentation is incomplete or inconsistent, the issue is not limited to recordkeeping. It reflects how decisions are being made.
