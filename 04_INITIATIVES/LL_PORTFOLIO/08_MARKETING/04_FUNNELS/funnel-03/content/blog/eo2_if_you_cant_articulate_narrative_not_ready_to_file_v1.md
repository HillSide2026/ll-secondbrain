# If You Can't Articulate the Narrative, You're Not Ready to File

Every STR decision depends on a narrative, whether it is written clearly or not.

The question is whether that narrative can be articulated.

A defensible narrative explains what was observed, how it was interpreted, and why it led to suspicion. It connects facts to a conclusion in a way that can be followed by someone else.

If that connection cannot be expressed clearly, the decision is not fully formed.

This does not mean that every detail must be captured. It means that the reasoning must be coherent.

When narratives are unclear, decisions appear inconsistent. Similar situations may be handled differently because the underlying reasoning is not stable or visible.

This becomes a problem when decisions are reviewed later. Without a clear narrative, it is difficult to explain why a report was filed or why it was not.

Narrative clarity is therefore not a documentation issue. It is a decision-making issue.

If the reasoning cannot be articulated, it cannot be applied consistently.
