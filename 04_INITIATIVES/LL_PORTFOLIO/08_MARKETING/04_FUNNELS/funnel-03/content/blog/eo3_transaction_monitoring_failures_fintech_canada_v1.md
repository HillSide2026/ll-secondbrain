# Transaction Monitoring Failures in Fintech (Canada)

Transaction monitoring systems rarely fail because they are absent. They fail because they do not reflect how the business actually operates.

Most fintech companies implement monitoring early. Rules are configured, thresholds are set, alerts are generated. The system appears complete. Over time, however, the business evolves while the monitoring logic does not.

This is where failures begin.

## Where Monitoring Breaks

Monitoring systems are designed around assumptions. Those assumptions include expected transaction size, frequency, customer behavior, and geographic exposure.

As the product changes, those assumptions become less accurate.

New payment flows are introduced. Transaction velocity increases. Customer profiles shift. Cross-border activity expands. The monitoring system continues to evaluate activity based on an earlier model.

At that point, the system is no longer identifying risk. It is applying outdated logic to new behavior.

## Under-Detection and Over-Detection

Failures typically appear in two forms.

In some cases, relevant activity is not flagged at all. Transactions that should raise questions pass through the system without escalation. This is usually the result of rules that are too narrow or no longer aligned with how the product functions.

In other cases, the system generates large volumes of alerts that do not correspond to meaningful risk. These alerts are reviewed quickly, often with minimal analysis, and resolved without consistent reasoning.

Both outcomes point to the same issue. The system is not calibrated to the business.

## The Problem of Static Rules

Monitoring rules are often implemented as fixed conditions. They are rarely revisited with the same rigor as the initial setup.

As a result, the system becomes static while the business becomes dynamic.

This creates a widening gap between:

- what the system is designed to detect
- what the business is actually doing

That gap is not immediately visible. It becomes visible when specific transactions are reviewed under scrutiny.

## Inconsistent Alert Handling

Even where alerts are generated, the way they are handled often lacks consistency.

Different reviewers may interpret similar activity differently. Escalation thresholds may be unclear. Decisions may depend more on context or judgment than on defined criteria.

This inconsistency becomes a problem when decisions need to be explained later.

If similar transactions are treated differently without a clear rationale, the issue is not the individual decision. It is the reliability of the system.

## The Link to Suspicious Transaction Reporting

Monitoring is not an isolated function. It feeds directly into suspicious transaction reporting.

If monitoring fails to identify relevant activity, reporting obligations may not be triggered at all.

If monitoring generates excessive noise, meaningful signals may be overlooked or deprioritized.

In both cases, the issue extends beyond monitoring. It affects how the business fulfills its reporting obligations.

## How Failures Are Identified

Monitoring failures are rarely discovered through internal review alone.

They are typically identified when:

- a bank asks how certain transactions were assessed
- a regulator reviews a sample of activity
- internal escalation reveals inconsistencies

At that point, the question is not whether monitoring exists. It is whether it produces outcomes that can be justified.

## The Practical Takeaway

Transaction monitoring in fintech environments must evolve with the business.

Rules, thresholds, and escalation logic must reflect current transaction behavior, not historical assumptions.

Where monitoring systems remain static, they gradually lose their ability to identify risk in a meaningful way.

The issue is not the presence of controls. It is whether those controls still correspond to reality.
