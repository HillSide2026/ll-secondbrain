# When Do You Have to File an STR in Canada (FINTRAC Timing Explained)

The obligation to file a suspicious transaction report arises when there are reasonable grounds to suspect that a transaction is related to money laundering or terrorist financing.

The timing requirement is often described as "as soon as practicable."

In practice, this creates uncertainty.

## The Meaning of "As Soon as Practicable"

The standard does not impose a fixed deadline. It requires action without unreasonable delay.

This allows for internal review and analysis, but not indefinite investigation.

The expectation is that once suspicion is formed, reporting follows within a timeframe that reflects the nature of the activity.

## Internal Review vs Delay

Businesses often delay reporting while gathering additional information.

Some level of review is appropriate. The issue arises when review becomes a substitute for decision-making.

The threshold is suspicion, not confirmation. Waiting for certainty can result in late reporting.

## Escalation Processes

Timing is closely tied to internal escalation.

If escalation paths are unclear or inconsistent, decisions take longer. This increases the risk of delay.

Clear escalation structures reduce uncertainty and support timely reporting.

## Consistency Across Cases

Similar situations should result in similar timelines.

If comparable transactions are reported at different speeds without a clear reason, this may indicate inconsistency in how the obligation is applied.

## The Practical Takeaway

The obligation to file an STR is triggered by suspicion, not proof.

Once that threshold is met, reporting should follow without unnecessary delay.

The challenge is not understanding the rule. It is applying it consistently and within a reasonable timeframe.
