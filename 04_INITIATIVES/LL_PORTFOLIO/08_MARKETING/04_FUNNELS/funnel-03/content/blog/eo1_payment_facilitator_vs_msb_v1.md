# Payment Facilitator vs MSB in Canada: Where the Line Actually Is

A payment facilitator is not a defined category under Canadian AML law. A money services business (MSB) is.

That distinction matters. Many companies describe themselves as payment facilitators and assume that classification determines their regulatory status. It does not. In Canada, the question is whether the underlying activity falls within the functional triggers that require MSB registration.

In practice, payment facilitators frequently operate in structures that meet those triggers.

## What a Payment Facilitator Actually Does

A payment facilitator typically sits between merchants and the underlying payment rails.

The model often includes:

- onboarding merchants onto a platform
- enabling those merchants to accept payments
- coordinating settlement through an acquiring bank or processor

From a product perspective, this looks like infrastructure. From a regulatory perspective, it can look like intermediation in the movement of funds.

That is where the analysis begins.

## Why the Label "Payment Facilitator" Is Not Determinative

Canadian AML rules do not classify businesses based on industry terminology. They classify based on activity.

Calling a business a payment facilitator does not answer the relevant question, which is whether the business is:

- receiving funds
- transmitting funds
- or facilitating the transfer of value between parties

If the answer to any of those is yes, the business may fall within MSB scope regardless of how it is positioned commercially.

## The Core Issue: Control Over the Payment Flow

The line between a payment facilitator and an MSB is not a formal boundary. It is a functional one, and it turns largely on control.

Where a business exercises meaningful control over the movement of funds—whether through routing logic, settlement timing, or merchant onboarding—it begins to resemble a money transmission model.

Where that control is minimal or fully outsourced, the analysis may point the other way.

This is not a binary distinction. Many real-world models sit somewhere in between.

## Common Structural Models

In practice, three broad structures tend to appear.

In a fully outsourced model, the facilitator provides a front-end interface but relies entirely on a regulated payment partner for onboarding, transaction processing, and settlement. In this structure, the facilitator may avoid MSB classification, assuming it does not independently control the flow of funds.

In a sponsored model, the facilitator operates under the umbrella of a regulated entity, but still plays an active role in merchant onboarding or transaction logic. This creates ambiguity. Regulatory responsibility may not be fully displaced, particularly if the facilitator retains operational control over key elements of the flow.

In a more independent model, the facilitator effectively manages how funds move between participants, even if third-party processors are used in the background. This structure often aligns more closely with MSB activity, especially where the facilitator determines how and when funds are transmitted.

The presence of a bank or processor in any of these models does not, on its own, resolve the analysis.

## Merchant Onboarding as a Risk Indicator

Merchant onboarding is frequently treated as a commercial function. In a regulatory context, it is a signal of control.

Where a facilitator:

- selects or approves merchants
- defines onboarding criteria
- maintains the merchant relationship

…it may be assuming responsibilities that align with regulated activity.

This is particularly relevant where onboarding decisions affect access to payment flows.

## Settlement Logic and Timing

Another critical factor is how settlement is structured.

If a facilitator determines:

- when merchants are paid
- how funds are split or routed
- whether funds are held, delayed, or aggregated

…it is exercising control over the movement of funds, even without formal custody.

This type of control is often sufficient to bring a business within MSB analysis.

## Where Misclassification Typically Happens

The most common mistake is assuming that reliance on an acquiring bank or payment processor eliminates regulatory exposure.

In reality, those relationships often address infrastructure, not classification.

If the facilitator designs the flow, controls participation, or determines how transactions are executed, it may still be performing functions that fall within MSB definitions.

A second mistake is focusing on contractual allocation of responsibility rather than operational reality. Agreements may assign compliance obligations to a partner, but regulators will look at how the system actually functions in practice.

## Canadian Nexus Still Applies

Even where a payment facilitator is based outside Canada, MSB obligations can still arise if the business:

- serves Canadian merchants or users
- enables transactions involving Canada
- directs its services into the Canadian market

This is often identified during banking onboarding or expansion planning, rather than at initial product design.

## When the Line Needs to Be Drawn Clearly

In simple models, classification may be relatively clear. In more complex structures, it is not.

A more detailed assessment is typically required where:

- multiple parties share responsibility for onboarding and settlement
- transaction flows are configurable or dynamic
- cross-border elements are embedded in the product
- external stakeholders require a definitive position

At that point, the question is not just whether MSB registration may be required, but whether the current structure can be defended under scrutiny.

## The Practical Takeaway

The distinction between a payment facilitator and an MSB is not a matter of terminology. It is a matter of function.

Where a business meaningfully participates in the movement of funds—particularly through control over onboarding, routing, or settlement—it is likely operating within the zone that Canadian AML rules are designed to capture.

The only reliable way to assess that exposure is to map the transaction flow in detail and evaluate where control actually sits.

That exercise tends to produce a clearer answer than any label.
