# Corporate Health Check for Ontario Businesses: How to Identify Legal Risk Before It Surfaces

Most legal issues in a business are not created at the moment they are discovered.

They already exist.

They sit in documents that no longer reflect reality, in relationships that have evolved without being restructured, and in decisions that were made informally but never documented properly.

The issue is not absence.

It is misalignment.

## Where Misalignment Comes From

Businesses evolve faster than their legal structure.

Shareholder roles change. Contractor relationships become employment relationships. Contracts are signed under time pressure. Corporate records fall behind actual decision-making.

Each change is manageable on its own.

Over time, they accumulate.

The legal framework begins to describe a version of the business that no longer exists.

## Why Problems Appear Suddenly

Legal risk rarely appears gradually.

It appears when something forces the structure to be tested.

A transaction begins and due diligence starts.

A shareholder relationship breaks down.

A contractor makes a claim.

A counterparty enforces a contract.

At that point, the question is not what the business intended.

It is what the documents support.

## The Limits of Reactive Review

Most legal review is reactive.

An issue arises, and a specific document or situation is examined.

This approach addresses symptoms.

It does not identify how issues connect across the business.

A shareholder agreement may be misaligned. Employment relationships may be misclassified. Contracts may allocate risk in ways that conflict with actual operations.

Looking at each in isolation misses the system-level problem.

## What a Corporate Health Check Actually Does

A corporate health check is not a document review.

It is a structural analysis.

It looks at how the business actually operates and compares that to corporate structure, ownership and control, contractual risk allocation, employment relationships, and regulatory exposure.

The objective is not completeness.

It is to identify where the structure does not match reality.

## The Five Areas Where Issues Concentrate

Across most mid-sized businesses, issues tend to cluster.

Ownership and control drift from what is documented.

Employment and contractor relationships no longer reflect legal classification.

Contracts allocate risk in ways the business cannot absorb.

Corporate records do not match actual decision-making.

Preparation for transactions is incomplete or inconsistent.

These are not isolated failures.

They are connected.

## What the Output Looks Like

The result is not a memo.

It is a map. Where risk exists. Where documents conflict. Where structure needs to change.

Some issues require immediate attention. Others are sequencing decisions.

The value is not in identifying everything.

It is in identifying what matters first.

## When This Becomes Relevant

Most businesses reach this point at a trigger moment.

Growth creates complexity. A transaction is contemplated. A dispute exposes gaps.

At that stage, the question is no longer whether issues exist.

It is how many, and where.

## The Practical Takeaway

A corporate health check is not about creating new documents.

It is about determining whether the existing structure still works.

Where it does not, the issue is not theoretical.

It is already embedded in the business.
