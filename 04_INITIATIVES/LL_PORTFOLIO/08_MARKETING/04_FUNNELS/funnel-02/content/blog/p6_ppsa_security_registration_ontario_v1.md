# PPSA Security in Ontario: What It Means When a Lender Registers Against Your Business

If a lender registers a PPSA security interest against your business, it means they have claimed legal rights over your assets to secure a loan or obligation.

In Ontario, this is done under the Personal Property Security Act. The registration itself does not mean you are in default. It means a lender has taken steps to protect its position if a default occurs.

Most business owners only encounter this system when they search their company and discover a registration, or when a new lender raises it during financing. At that point, the question is not what the PPSA is in theory. The question is what this specific registration means for your business.

## What a PPSA Registration Actually Does

A PPSA registration is a public notice. It tells other creditors that a lender has a security interest in the borrower's personal property.

In most commercial lending transactions, the underlying document is a general security agreement. That agreement gives the lender rights over a broad category of assets. The PPSA registration makes those rights visible and establishes priority.

From a practical standpoint, the registration affects three things. It determines who has first claim over assets, it restricts the borrower's ability to grant overlapping security, and it shapes what happens if the business defaults.

## What Assets Are Covered

In many Ontario loan transactions, the security is not limited to a single asset. It is drafted broadly.

A typical general security agreement will cover equipment, inventory, accounts receivable, cash, and intangible assets. In some cases, it will also include after-acquired property, meaning assets the business obtains after the agreement is signed.

The result is that one registration can effectively encumber most of the operating assets of the business.

This is often not obvious to the borrower at the time of signing. It becomes relevant later, when trying to obtain additional financing or when reviewing existing obligations.

## Why Lenders Register

Registration is about priority, not control.

If multiple lenders have claims against the same borrower, the order of registration can determine who gets paid first from the available assets. A lender that fails to register risks losing priority to another creditor who does.

For that reason, registration is standard in secured lending. It is not a signal that something is wrong with the business. It is a baseline protection for the lender.

## What It Means for New Financing

A PPSA registration can complicate future borrowing.

A new lender will search the PPSA registry as part of its due diligence. If it finds an existing registration, it will want to understand the scope of that security and whether it conflicts with the new financing.

In some cases, the existing lender must agree to subordinate its interest or limit its security. In other cases, the registration must be amended or discharged.

If this is not addressed early, it can delay or block a transaction that would otherwise proceed.

## Discharging a PPSA Registration

A PPSA registration does not disappear automatically when a loan is repaid.

The secured party is responsible for discharging the registration. If that step is missed, the registration can remain on record and continue to affect the borrower's ability to obtain financing.

In practice, this creates situations where a business has paid off a loan but still appears encumbered in the registry. Resolving that requires identifying the original secured party and completing the discharge process.

## What Happens on Default

The registration itself does not create enforcement rights. Those rights come from the underlying security agreement.

However, once a default occurs, the registration allows the lender to act against the secured assets. This can include taking possession, appointing a receiver, or otherwise realizing on the collateral.

Because the security is often broad, enforcement can affect most of the business's operations.

## Where Businesses Run Into Problems

The common issue is not the existence of a registration. It is a lack of clarity about what it covers and how it interacts with other obligations.

Businesses often discover registrations when preparing for new financing or a sale. At that point, questions arise about priority, scope, and whether prior obligations were properly discharged.

Those questions are harder to resolve when a transaction is already in progress and timelines are tight.

## Why This Matters Before You Borrow Again

A PPSA registration is part of the legal infrastructure of your business. It sits alongside your corporate records, shareholder structure, and existing contracts.

When a new lender enters the picture, all of these elements are reviewed together. If the registration does not align cleanly with the current state of the business, it becomes an issue to resolve under time pressure.

Understanding what has been registered, and why, puts you in a better position before that process begins.
