# Multiple Agreements, No Single Structure: When Documents Conflict

Most businesses do not operate under a single governing document.

They operate under layers.

A shareholder agreement defines ownership and control. Articles and by-laws define corporate structure. Employment agreements define individual relationships. Commercial contracts allocate risk externally.

Each document may have been drafted correctly at the time.

They are rarely reviewed together.

## Where Conflict Develops

Conflict does not require contradiction on its face.

It arises when documents operate on different assumptions.

A shareholder agreement may restrict share transfers. The articles may permit them. An employment agreement may promise equity that does not align with the share structure.

Individually, each document appears functional.

Together, they describe different versions of the business.

## Which Document Controls

When documents conflict, the outcome is not determined by intent.

It is determined by structure.

Corporate documents may override contractual expectations. External agreements may create obligations that internal documents do not anticipate.

The result is not always obvious in advance.

## When This Becomes Visible

These conflicts remain dormant until tested.

A transfer is attempted.

A termination occurs.

A transaction requires diligence.

At that point, the documents are read together for the first time.

What emerges is not alignment, but inconsistency.

## The Practical Takeaway

The issue is not whether each document works on its own.

It is whether they operate as a coherent system.

A health check identifies where documents diverge before those divergences determine the outcome.
