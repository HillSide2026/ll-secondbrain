# Private Mortgage Lending in Ontario: Legal Requirements for Lenders and Borrowers

Private mortgage lending in Ontario is often positioned as a faster alternative to bank financing. That is accurate in terms of access. It is not accurate in terms of legal simplicity.

A private mortgage is still a secured loan. It is documented, registered, and enforceable. The difference is that the parties are not operating within a standardized institutional framework. That shifts more responsibility onto the documentation itself.

For both borrowers and lenders, the structure of that documentation determines how the transaction operates in practice.

## What Defines a Private Mortgage

A private mortgage is a loan secured against real property where the lender is not a traditional financial institution.

The lender may be an individual, a group of investors, or an entity structured for lending purposes. The borrower may be an individual or a corporation, including an operating business using real estate as collateral.

The core elements are consistent. There is a mortgage registered against the property, a loan agreement or commitment setting out terms, and often additional documents addressing fees, guarantees, and default.

## Documentation in a Private Mortgage Transaction

The legal structure typically includes a mortgage registered on title, supporting loan terms, and, in many cases, personal or corporate guarantees.

Unlike institutional lending, the allocation of terms across these documents can vary. Fees may appear in multiple places. Default provisions may be broader or triggered more easily. Rights on enforcement may be drafted with fewer constraints.

Because there is no uniform template, the meaning of the transaction sits in how these documents interact.

## Registration and Priority

The mortgage is registered on title to the property. This establishes the lender's priority relative to other claims.

Priority determines who gets paid first if the property is sold under enforcement. In transactions involving multiple mortgages, the order of registration is critical.

For borrowers, this affects refinancing and the ability to layer additional financing. For lenders, it determines the level of risk associated with the loan.

## Default and Enforcement

Enforcement in private mortgage lending is typically faster than in institutional settings.

If a default occurs, the lender may rely on the remedies set out in the mortgage and related documents. This can include power of sale or other enforcement mechanisms available under Ontario law.

Because timelines and protections can differ from bank lending, the borrower's position depends heavily on how default is defined and what notice periods apply.

## Regulatory Considerations

Private mortgage lending in Ontario exists within a regulatory framework, but it is not governed in the same way as bank lending.

Certain participants may be subject to licensing or oversight, depending on how the lending activity is structured. However, for many transactions, the primary protections are contractual rather than regulatory.

That makes the documentation more significant. The agreement between the parties does most of the work.

## Where Borrowers Misjudge Risk

Borrowers often focus on speed and access. That is usually the reason the transaction exists.

What is less visible is how the legal terms affect control and downside.

Interest rates are only one component. Fees, default triggers, and enforcement rights can change the economic outcome of the loan in ways that are not obvious at a high level.

Because the process moves quickly, these elements are often reviewed under time pressure.

## Where Lenders Misjudge Risk

For private lenders, the risk is not just borrower default. It is enforceability.

If documentation is inconsistent, incomplete, or poorly structured, enforcing the mortgage can become more complex than expected. Priority issues, unclear terms, or gaps in supporting documents can reduce recovery.

As lending activity becomes more frequent or structured, these issues become more material.

## Why the Documentation Matters

A private mortgage is not defined by the fact that it is private. It is defined by how it is documented.

For both borrowers and lenders, the agreement determines how the transaction operates from funding through enforcement.

In Ontario, where private lending is common in certain segments of the market, the difference between a workable transaction and a problematic one is often not the deal itself. It is how the deal is recorded.
