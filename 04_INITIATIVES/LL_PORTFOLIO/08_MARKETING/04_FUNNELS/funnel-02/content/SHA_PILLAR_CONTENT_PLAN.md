## Framing Note

These posts are written for the Ontario business owner ($1M–$8M), not for counsel. The source material includes lawyer-to-lawyer drafting failure analysis — that framing must be translated. The reader is a founder, CEO, or co-owner who signed a shareholder agreement years ago and hasn't looked at it since. They are not in crisis. They are becoming aware of exposure.

The "Ten Drafting Failures" material is **not** in this plan — it belongs on LinkedIn as professional thought leadership for referral sources (accountants, other lawyers), not as SEO content for business owners.

## Post 3 — Death, Disability, Insolvency

## Post 4 — Share Valuation

**Title:** "Fair Market Value as Determined by Agreement" Is Not a Valuation Clause
**Target keyword:** `private company share valuation ontario` / `shareholder buyout valuation dispute ontario`
**Intent:** Problem-aware — owner has been told or suspects valuation is unclear
**Competition:** Low direct competition on the business-owner-framing side. Most content is accounting/valuation-firm focused.
**Angle:**
- The line "fair market value as determined by agreement" is effectively an invitation to litigate
- What a real valuation clause requires: methodology (EBITDA multiple, book value, DCF), who determines it, how disagreements are resolved, fallback to independent CBV
- The cost asymmetry: a poorly drafted clause costs more in legal fees than a properly drafted one costs to fix
- Practical example: two shareholders disagree by $500K on FMV; no mechanism means no path forward without litigation
**Bridge to offer:** Valuation mechanism review is part of the shareholder structure audit in the Corporate Health Check.
**Source material:** Item 5 (valuation formulas without dispute mechanisms) + Item 4 (valuation procedure — CBV, methodology, fallback)

## Post 5 — Founder Fired, Keeps Shares

**Title:** What Happens to a Founder's Shares When Their Employment Ends in Ontario
**Target keyword:** `founder shares employment terminated ontario` / `shareholder fired keeps shares ontario`
**Intent:** Specific scenario — owner has a co-founder situation and is thinking about what happens if the relationship breaks down
**Competition:** Very low. Almost no business-owner-facing content on this specific intersection. Most content is either pure employment law (severance, wrongful dismissal) or pure corporate (share transfers). The gap between the two is underserved.
**Angle:**
- The two-hat problem: a founder is simultaneously a shareholder and an employee. Terminating the employment doesn't automatically touch the shares.
- The hostage situation: a terminated co-founder retains full voting rights, information rights, and pre-emptive rights unless the shareholder agreement explicitly addresses it
- What coordinated provisions look like: vesting tied to employment, retraction rights on departure, buyback triggers, pricing at departure vs. current FMV
- Why this needs to be drafted in both the SHA and the employment agreement together
**Bridge to offer:** Employment and shareholder structure alignment is flagged in the Corporate Health Check review.
**Source material:** Item 9 (employment/shareholder intersection) + Item 1 (vesting/employment coordination)

## Post 7 — USA vs. Regular Shareholder Agreement

**Title:** Is Your Shareholder Agreement a Unanimous Shareholder Agreement? The Distinction Ontario Business Owners Need to Understand
**Target keyword:** `unanimous shareholder agreement ontario` / `USA OBCA ontario`
**Intent:** Education — owner has been told there might be a problem, or their accountant or bank has flagged it
**Competition:** Lower volume but higher-quality audience. Technical topic but searchable by business owners who have been specifically flagged. Most content is lawyer-to-lawyer.
**Angle:**
- The difference: a regular SHA governs relationships between shareholders; a USA under s. 146 OBCA strips powers from directors and gives them to shareholders — with a direct consequence
- The consequence: shareholders who assume director powers under a USA inherit director-level liability under OBCA s. 146(5)
- The drafting failure: agreements that strip director powers informally without tracking clause-by-clause what was transferred and what wasn't
- The labelling problem: an agreement called a "shareholder agreement" that functions as a USA — courts will look at substance, not label
- When a USA is appropriate and when it isn't
**Bridge to offer:** USA vs. non-USA distinction is part of the governance integrity review in the Corporate Health Check.
**Source material:** Item 1 (director vs. shareholder powers, OBCA framing) + Item 8 (USA without proper power transfer, s. 146(5) liability)
