# F02 Referral Channel Research
## Corporate Health Check — Selling Through Professional Referral Networks

**Scope:** Ontario. ICP: owners of private companies doing $10M–$100M+ in annual revenue (7–8 figures). This ICP sits above the current F02 baseline ($1M–$8M) and justifies a distinct referral-channel positioning strategy.

---

## Framing: What "Offer" Means Here

In this analysis, "offer" means the **end outcome the client is seeking** — the problem they are trying to solve or the state they want to reach — that makes the Corporate Health Check relevant to them. The CHC is the vehicle; the offer is the destination.

For 7–8 figure private company owners in Ontario, those destinations cluster around four themes:

- **Transaction Readiness** — being ready to sell, finance, or bring in capital without friction
- **Governance Defensibility** — having documentation that holds up under external scrutiny
- **Succession Clarity** — having a structure that can outlive the founder or accommodate ownership transition
- **Risk Containment** — knowing where the structural exposure is before it surfaces in a deal, dispute, or audit

Each referral channel activates a different subset of these.

---

## 1. Financial Planners

### Ontario Context

Financial planners serving business owners at the 7–8 figure level typically operate as wealth managers, fee-based advisors, or planners attached to private banking or family office platforms. Since March 2022, the Financial Professionals Title Protection Act (FSRA) requires anyone using the titles "Financial Planner" or "Financial Advisor" in Ontario to hold an approved credential (CFP, Qafp, or equivalent approved by FP Canada or FPSC).

At this revenue level, the relevant practitioner base includes:
- Certified Financial Planners (CFP) doing integrated wealth and business planning
- STEP Canada members (Society of Trust and Estate Practitioners) handling estate-integrated planning
- Private banking relationship managers at major financial institutions
- Independent family office advisors

The financial planner's role for a 7–8 figure business owner is to integrate personal wealth planning with the business structure — ensuring the business serves the owner's financial goals over time.

### Their Pain Points (Why They Would Refer)

**1. The plan depends on documents the planner has never read.**
A financial plan for a business owner is only as good as what the underlying corporate structure actually says. The planner models an estate freeze, a holdco distribution strategy, or a succession transfer — but if the shareholder agreement doesn't reflect current ownership, or the share structure has undocumented equity grants, the plan will fail on contact with reality. The planner has no mechanism to identify these gaps. They need a lawyer to verify the legal foundation before the plan is built on it.

**2. They can't advise on the corporate restructuring required to implement the plan.**
Estate planning for a 7–8 figure business owner routinely involves:
- Holding company structures (OpCo/HoldCo)
- Family trust integration
- Estate freezes (preferred share exchange)
- Succession share issuances to next generation

All of these require legal implementation. The financial planner identifies the need; they cannot execute it. When the corporate records are out of date, the implementation hits complications they cannot resolve. A CHC surfaces what needs to be cleaned up before the planner's restructuring can proceed.

**3. Buy-sell planning is incomplete without legal alignment.**
Financial planners routinely identify that a client needs buy-sell insurance. But the buy-sell mechanism (funded or unfunded) must align with what the shareholders agreement actually says about redemption, purchase triggers, and valuation. At the 7–8 figure level, this is often a multi-million dollar mismatch. The planner is exposed professionally if the insurance structure doesn't match the legal structure.

**4. Succession planning without legal succession is a proposal, not a plan.**
The client wants to transition the business to their children or a management team within 5–10 years. The financial planner can model the wealth transfer. But if the legal governance structure, employment agreements, and authority documentation don't support the transition, the plan is theoretical. The planner needs a lawyer partner who can assess whether the legal architecture supports succession.

**5. LCGE exposure is a legal question, not just an accounting one.**
At the 7–8 figure level, the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE — now approximately $1.25M under current provisions, indexed annually) is a major planning element. But LCGE eligibility depends on the shares meeting the Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) conditions under the Income Tax Act — conditions that have legal implications (holding period, active business test, asset tests). The financial planner knows the target but cannot confirm the current share structure qualifies.

### Relevant Offers (What the Client Seeks Through the CHC)

| Offer / End Goal | How CHC Delivers It |
|---|---|
| **Succession-ready structure** | CHC confirms the governance, ownership, and authority documentation supports a planned transition — so the succession plan is legally executable, not just financially modeled |
| **Estate plan that holds** | CHC identifies corporate structural issues that would complicate estate administration (improperly issued shares, undocumented equity expectations, stale trusts) before the estate plan is finalized |
| **Clean holdco/opco structure** | CHC verifies that the corporate structure (especially after prior accountant-driven reorganizations) is legally sound and that documents reflect the current structure |
| **Personal liability clarity** | CHC flags director and officer liability exposure that the financial planner cannot assess — relevant for the personal wealth protection goals of the owner |
| **Transaction-ready business** | If the owner contemplates a future sale, CHC provides the pre-sale structural clarity that will be required in due diligence anyway — done proactively, at lower cost |

### Referral Pitch Summary (Financial Planner)

> "Before we build the plan, let's confirm the legal architecture supports it. A Corporate Health Check reviews the corporate structure, ownership documentation, and governance records — and tells us where there are misalignments we need to resolve before we proceed. It's a fixed-fee diagnostic. It gives us the legal foundation to work from."

## 2. Accountants

### Ontario Context

CPAs in Ontario (CPA Ontario, formed by the merger of ICAO, CMAAO, and CGAAO) serving owner-managed businesses at the 7–8 figure level are the single most powerful referral channel. They are:
- The most trusted advisor relationship in the business
- The first to see the corporate structure (through tax filings, financial statements, and corporate tax returns — T2s)
- Often the architect of the corporate structure through tax-driven reorganizations
- Frequently aware of legal gaps but professionally constrained from addressing them

CPA firms serving this ICP range from mid-size regional firms with strong owner-managed practices to the national firms' private company groups (BDO, MNP, DMCL, KPMG Enterprise, etc.).

### Their Pain Points (Why They Would Refer)

**1. They've built a tax structure on a legal foundation they've never verified.**
The CPA has recommended, or implemented, a holding company structure, an estate freeze, or an income-splitting arrangement. They know the tax outcome they're trying to achieve. What they often don't know is whether the legal documents — the shareholder agreement, the articles of incorporation, the share structure — actually reflect what they filed. If the legal structure doesn't match the tax position, the client is exposed.

**2. CRA audit risk is amplified by documentation gaps.**
At 7–8 figures, T2 review and transfer pricing scrutiny are more common. The CRA may challenge:
- Reasonableness of management fees between OpCo and HoldCo
- Validity of dividend extractions
- Shareholder loan treatment
- TOSI (Tax on Split Income) compliance under s. 120.4 ITA

The CPA can defend the tax position. But if the underlying corporate records don't support the arrangements — minutes approving management fees, directors resolutions authorizing dividends, proper documentation of shareholder loans — the defense is harder. The CHC surfaces documentation gaps before the audit.

**3. LCGE qualification is a joint legal-accounting problem.**
For owners approaching a sale, the LCGE is often the single largest financial planning variable. LCGE on capital gains from the sale of QSBC shares requires:
- 24-month holding period for the shares
- 50% active business asset test at time of disposition
- 90% active business asset test throughout the 24-month period
- The shares must have been held by the taxpayer or a related person

These conditions have legal dimensions the CPA relies on the corporate structure to satisfy. If the shares were improperly issued, if there are unresolved option exercises, or if the corporate records are inconsistent — the LCGE claim is at risk. A CHC provides the legal confirmation the CPA needs.

**4. Transaction due diligence exposes what the CPA never found.**
When a 7–8 figure business goes to sale, M&A due diligence (whether asset or share purchase) will review:
- Corporate minute books
- Shareholder agreements
- Employment agreements (for change of control triggers)
- Commercial contracts (assignment and change of control clauses)
- Regulatory compliance

The CPA is in the room when the deal falls apart because of a legal issue that should have been resolved before the buyer showed up. The CHC is the referral that prevents that outcome — and the CPA looks better for having made it.

**5. Post-reorganization cleanup.**
CPAs frequently implement corporate reorganizations — purification transactions (to clean up non-active assets before sale), estate freezes, share subscription transactions — that create new legal obligations (resolutions, consents, updated registers). These are often not followed up legally. The minute book doesn't reflect the reorganization. The CHC identifies and remediates these gaps.

**6. Management compensation structure is legally fragile.**
At 7–8 figures, compensation for owner-managers typically involves a combination of salary, dividend, and shareholder loan repayments. The CPA structures these for tax efficiency. But if the employment arrangements, director roles, or shareholder agreements don't properly authorize and document these arrangements, the tax position is weaker and the owner has personal exposure. The CHC confirms whether the documentation supports the compensation strategy.

### Relevant Offers (What the Client Seeks Through the CHC)

| Offer / End Goal | How CHC Delivers It |
|---|---|
| **LCGE-ready share structure** | CHC confirms shares meet QSBC conditions and that the corporate records support the holding period and asset test requirements |
| **Sale-ready corporate structure** | CHC surfaces and remediates the issues that M&A due diligence will find — done before the process starts, not during it |
| **CRA-defensible records** | CHC remediates minute book gaps, management fee documentation, and director authorization records before a CRA review |
| **Reorganization confirmation** | CHC confirms that the legal structure as reorganized matches the tax filings — closing the loop on accountant-led restructuring |
| **Clean exit** | At 7–8 figures, the ultimate offer: the owner sells, keeps the LCGE, and has no deal friction from legal structure problems |

### Referral Pitch Summary (Accountant)

> "We've structured this well from a tax perspective. Before you go to market — or before CRA has a look — it's worth having a lawyer confirm that the legal documents actually reflect the structure we've been filing. A Corporate Health Check does that. It's a fixed-fee diagnostic, and it gives us both confidence that the documents support the plan."

## 3. Insurance Brokers

### Ontario Context

The insurance broker referral channel is more segmented than accountants or financial planners:

- **Life insurance agents/advisors** (FSRA-licensed): The most relevant for CHC referrals. This group includes independent life agents and those working through MGAs (Managing General Agents like PPI, IDC Worldsource, Equitable Life). CALU (Conference for Advanced Life Underwriting) members are the most sophisticated sub-set — they specialize in complex business owner insurance planning.

- **P&C brokers** (RIBO-registered): Commercial lines brokers placing D&O, commercial general liability, and business interruption. Less likely to refer to CHC directly, but relevant where they are doing coverage reviews that surface governance gaps.

At the 7–8 figure level, the relevant insurance planning products include:
- Corporate-owned life insurance (COLI) / permanent life insurance for tax-exempt accumulation
- Buy-sell life insurance (cross-purchase or redemption structures)
- Key person insurance
- Disability insurance for business owners (business overhead expense, critical illness)
- Directors and Officers (D&O) liability coverage

### Their Pain Points (Why They Would Refer)

**1. The buy-sell insurance doesn't match the shareholders agreement.**
This is the single most common trigger. A life agent sells a buy-sell insurance policy — often structured so that each shareholder's life is insured, with proceeds going to fund the purchase of the deceased/departing shareholder's shares. The policy is placed. The premium is paid. But:

- The shareholders agreement was drafted 10 years ago and doesn't reflect current ownership
- The valuation mechanism in the agreement doesn't match the coverage amount
- The triggering events in the agreement (death, disability, voluntary departure) don't align with what the policy covers
- One or more shareholders have been added or have increased their ownership without the agreement being updated

At the 7–8 figure level, this is a multi-million dollar exposure. The broker placed the policy in good faith. If the trigger event occurs and the legal documents don't support the intended outcome, the broker has a professional liability problem. A CHC that reviews the shareholders agreement and flags the misalignment is a direct risk mitigation for the broker.

**2. Corporate-owned life insurance requires documented corporate authority.**
Corporate-owned life insurance at the 7–8 figure level involves the corporation as owner and beneficiary of a life insurance policy. This requires:
- Proper director resolutions authorizing the policy purchase
- Proper documentation of premium payments (especially if some personal benefit is attributed)
- Corporate minutes reflecting the insurance decision

If the minute book is not maintained, or the resolutions don't exist, the broker's product placement is structurally unsupported. The CHC remediates this.

**3. Key person insurance valuation depends on defensible ownership documentation.**
Underwriting key person insurance at this level involves establishing:
- The key person's ownership interest (and what happens if they leave)
- The company's valuation method (a multiple of EBITDA, book value, etc.)
- Whether the departing key person creates a triggering event under any existing agreements

If ownership is informally structured, or if the shareholders agreement doesn't address key person departure, the underwriter and the insured are working from different assumptions. The CHC surfaces what the documents actually say.

**4. D&O insurance underwriting reveals governance gaps.**
Commercial P&C brokers placing D&O coverage for a 7–8 figure private company need to understand the company's governance. Underwriters will ask about:
- Whether the board has independent directors
- Whether minutes are maintained
- Whether there are outstanding shareholder disputes or claims

A D&O application that reveals governance deficiencies (no minutes for years, informal director appointments, officer roles not documented) creates underwriting friction. A CHC done before the application cleans up the records and supports better coverage placement.

**5. Business continuity insurance planning requires a legal continuity framework.**
Business interruption and continuity insurance assumes the business can continue operating. But if the legal governance structure is unclear — authority is concentrated in one person with no succession documentation, employment agreements have no assignment clauses, customer contracts have triggering change-of-control provisions — the insured event (the disruption) will surface legal problems the insurer can't fix. The broker's product is more effective when the underlying legal structure is coherent.

### Relevant Offers (What the Client Seeks Through the CHC)

| Offer / End Goal | How CHC Delivers It |
|---|---|
| **Buy-sell mechanism that actually works** | CHC reviews and aligns the shareholders agreement with the insurance structure — so the policy trigger events, valuation mechanisms, and ownership provisions are consistent |
| **Documented corporate authority for COLI** | CHC confirms and remediates the director resolutions and corporate record-keeping that support corporate-owned life insurance |
| **Key person risk reduction** | CHC documents authority, succession provisions, and departure mechanisms — reducing the informality that makes key person risk opaque |
| **Governance defensibility for D&O** | CHC remediates governance record gaps that affect D&O underwriting and claims defence |
| **Business continuity legal readiness** | CHC identifies contractual and governance provisions that affect the company's ability to survive a triggering disruption |

### Referral Pitch Summary (Insurance Broker / Life Agent)

> "The insurance structure only works if the shareholders agreement matches it. If the agreement is out of date, or if the corporate records don't support the arrangement, the policy proceeds are at risk at the moment they're needed most. A Corporate Health Check reviews exactly those documents and produces a prioritized action plan. It's a fixed-fee diagnostic — the cost is small compared to the coverage amounts we're placing."

## ICP Calibration Note: 7–8 Figure vs. Current F02 ICP

The current F02 ICP is $1M–$8M revenue, which is structurally a direct-to-owner inbound marketing play (content, lead magnet, Google Ads). The referral channel strategy targets a materially different segment:

| | Current F02 ICP | Referral Channel ICP |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $1M–$8M | $10M–$100M+ |
| Legal advisor relationship | Often reactive / episodic | More established, but still no in-house counsel |
| Financial planner | May or may not have one | Almost always engaged |
| Accountant | Involved, often not sophisticated | CPA firm with owner-managed practice |
| Insurance | Basic commercial coverage | Complex planning products in place |
| CHC price point | $2,500–$4,500 | $5,000–$15,000+ justified |
| Deal at stake | General structural hygiene | Specific transaction, succession, or tax event |

At 7–8 figures, the CHC should be positioned as a **pre-event** diagnostic tied to a specific near-term event (sale, succession, reorganization, financing) — not general preventative maintenance. The referral partner provides the event context; the CHC provides the legal readiness assessment.

## Implementation Considerations

1. **Accountant channel is highest-priority.** The CPA relationship is the most trusted and the most structurally motivated to refer. The LCGE / M&A due diligence angle is the strongest referral narrative.

2. **Life agent channel is a natural fit.** The buy-sell/shareholders agreement misalignment is a clear, specific, and verifiable problem. CALU-member life agents are the most sophisticated sub-segment to target.

3. **Financial planner channel is relationship-dependent.** Requires a planner who is doing integrated business/estate planning, not just investment management. The referral motivation is strong where succession planning is active.

4. **Referral partner positioning differs from client positioning.** The referral partner's pain is professional (their work product is compromised by legal gaps). The client's pain is outcome-based (they can't execute the transaction, succession, or plan without clean legal structure). Both messages need to exist.

5. **Price point at 7–8 figures.** A $2,500–$4,500 CHC underprices the risk and the opportunity at this revenue level. A $7,500–$15,000 range is defensible when tied to a specific event and delivered with commensurate depth. Referral partners at this level also need the fee to make sense relative to what they're placing or planning.
