### Industries We Serve

Not every business faces the same legal and structural questions. The issues a construction company works through when scaling are different from the issues a payments processor faces when expanding into Canada. A health clinic navigating employment and compliance has different structural priorities than a managed services firm building out its contract stack.

Industry context is not a background variable. It shapes the governance structure a business needs, the contracts that need to work, the regulatory exposure that must be managed, and the growth decisions that carry legal consequence.

Levine Law works with operating businesses in selected industries. Our advice is informed by the specific commercial, structural, and regulatory environment of each client's business — not by a generic playbook applied uniformly across sectors.

This page organizes the industries we work in, explains how industry context changes the legal work, and routes you to the more specific resources relevant to your situation.

### Why the Same Advice Doesn't Work Across Every Business

A corporate lawyer advising a construction company and one advising a payments operator are doing structurally different work. The underlying legal disciplines may overlap, but the priorities, risks, and practical issues are not interchangeable.

For a construction company with ten to thirty employees, the structural work often centers on the shareholder agreement, how the corporate structure is organized for growth and ownership transitions, how project contracts allocate risk between the company and its subcontractors, and whether the employment arrangements reflect how the company actually operates. The legal infrastructure is built around a project-heavy model with recurring commercial and governance decisions.

For a payments operator or money services business with Canadian exposure, the priorities are different. The regulatory framework requires specific registration and compliance functions — MSB registration under the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act, AML program design and maintenance, suspicious transaction reporting obligations under FINTRAC, and increasingly the reporting obligations under the Retail Payment Activities Act. The commercial contracts govern payment processing relationships and technology vendors, not job sites. The structural and regulatory problems are industry-specific and cannot be addressed with a general corporate law approach.

This is why Levine Law organizes its work by industry context. It affects what questions we ask, what documents we review, what structural gaps we flag, and what the useful advice looks like in practice.

### Common Legal and Structural Issues by Industry

The following are recurring issues we see across the industries we serve. These are not emergencies — they are the structural and regulatory realities that operating businesses in each sector need to manage proactively.

---

**Construction and Real Estate**

- Shareholder agreements that do not address exit, buy-sell, or decision-making for a company that has grown beyond its founding arrangements.
- Project contracts that inadequately allocate risk between the company and subcontractors, suppliers, or joint venture partners.
- Corporate structure that is not organized for holding real estate assets separately from operating risk.
- Employment arrangements with field staff or site supervisors that create classification risk.
- Ownership transitions — bringing in a new partner or preparing for a sale — without the governance documents to support them.

---

**Health and Wellness**

- Professional corporation structure that is not aligned with current ownership, employment, or partnership arrangements.
- Employment agreements for clinical staff that do not reflect the regulatory environment of the practice.
- Franchise or licensing agreements for clinic networks that have not been reviewed against the firm's legal obligations.
- Expansion into new service lines or locations without the corporate and compliance structure to support it.
- Shareholder or partner disputes in professional practice settings where the governance documents were never built for conflict.

---

**Managed Services and Technology**

- Master service agreements that do not adequately define scope, ownership of work product, or liability allocation.
- IP and work-for-hire provisions in contractor agreements that leave ownership unclear.
- Vendor contract stacks where obligations compound but no one has mapped the exposure.
- Corporate structure that was set up for a small team and has not been updated as the business scaled.
- Equity or compensation arrangements with early employees or contractors that were never properly documented.

---

**Payments, Fintech, MSB, and PSP Operators**

- MSB registration requirements under federal AML legislation that have not been assessed or completed.
- AML compliance programs that are not built to meet FINTRAC's requirements for the specific business model.
- Suspicious transaction reporting obligations that are not integrated into the company's operational workflow.
- RPAA reporting and registration requirements for payment service providers operating in Canada.
- Payment processing agreements that were negotiated without counsel and carry risk in dispute or termination scenarios.
- Corporate structure questions when entering the Canadian market — whether to establish a subsidiary, branch, or contracting arrangement, and what obligations attach to each.

---

**Food, Agriculture, and Consumer Goods**

- Supplier agreements that do not address pricing variability, supply disruption, or exclusivity in a way that protects the business.
- Distribution contracts that create dependency without adequate exit or renegotiation provisions.
- Corporate governance for businesses that have grown significantly but never updated their foundational documents.
- Franchise law considerations for businesses that have expanded through licensed or franchised models.
- Ownership and equity arrangements for businesses that have evolved past their founding partnership structure.

---

**Energy and Logistics**

- Large commercial contracts where risk allocation has not kept pace with the scale of the work.
- Joint venture or partnership structures for project-specific operations that require separate corporate vehicles.
- Corporate structure for businesses that operate across multiple legal entities without clear governance between them.
- Subcontractor and service agreements where terms were negotiated without adequate legal review.
- Ownership transition and succession planning for owner-operated businesses approaching a sale or generational handoff.
