# Strategic Editor Review — Industries Pillar Page Draft v1

## Reviewer Function

The Strategic Editor (SE-01) performs the final coherence gate before ML1 approval. This review assesses:

1. Whether the draft is coherent as a Level 2 pillar page — not a homepage, not a Level 3 page.
2. Whether it holds its structural purpose relative to the Services and Inquiries pages.
3. Whether the routing function to Level 3 pages is clear.
4. Whether tone and language is consistent and strong throughout.
5. Identification of any sections that are generic, weak, or misaligned.

---

## Overall Assessment

The draft is structurally sound. It reads as a Level 2 pillar page — educational, organizational, and routing-oriented — without slipping into homepage mode or Level 3 conversion behavior. The tone is consistent throughout. The ICP-02 coverage (Payments/Fintech/MSB/PSP) is present and substantive.

The primary strategic observation is about the page's positioning in the competitive landscape of the visitor's mental journey. The page earns the reader's attention through specificity — it should not surrender that specificity at any point. Two sections hold up well under this test. Two sections pass with minor notes. No section fails.

---

## Section-by-Section Strategic Assessment

---

### Section 1 — Intro / Why Industry Context Matters

**Coherence as Level 2:** STRONG.
The opening paragraph is the page's strategic thesis: "Not every business faces the same legal and structural questions." This is the correct frame for a Level 2 industry page. It immediately separates the page from a generic firm overview.

The construction vs. payments example in the first paragraph is well-chosen. These are two distinct ICPs, and placing them in contrast in the first paragraph signals to visitors from either group that the firm understands the difference — without requiring them to read further to find out if they are in scope.

**Routing function:** Clear. The final sentence of the section explicitly names what the page does: "This page organizes the industries we work in, explains how industry context changes the legal work, and routes you to the more specific resources relevant to your situation." This is a clean, honest description of a Level 2 pillar page's function.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. The section does not mention services, engagement models, or contact. It sets up the page's educational function only.

**Tone:** Consistent with the calm authority posture. The phrase "industry context is not a background variable" is analytically strong. It treats the reader as someone who can follow a structural argument.

**Strategic flag:** None. This section is the strongest anchor for the page.

---

### Section 2 — Industries We Serve

**Coherence as Level 2:** PASS.
The section delivers what it promises: a clean, scannable list of six industry categories. Each entry is one to two sentences and business-facing. The section does not slip into service descriptions.

**Routing function:** Partially explicit. The section has the right structure for Level 2 to Level 3 routing (one entry per industry, each linking forward), but the actual links are placeholder text "[Go to this page]" at the draft stage. This is expected — the Level 3 pages do not yet exist. The implementation architecture is correct.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. No services are described here. No contact path is offered.

**Tone:** Consistent. The descriptors are businesslike and grounded. No superlatives.

**Strategic observation:** The Energy and Logistics entry is the weakest of the six. "Complex operational footprints, large commercial contracts, and project-specific corporate structures define the legal infrastructure needs for operators in this sector" — this is accurate but slightly more general than the other five entries. The other five entries name specific structural or regulatory characteristics; this one names characteristics that could apply to multiple industries. The entry does not fail, but it could be strengthened by one concrete differentiator (e.g., reference to joint venture vehicles for project operations, or the specific contract scale issues for this sector). Non-blocking.

---

### Section 3 — How Our Advice Changes By Industry

**Coherence as Level 2:** STRONG.
This section is the strategic heart of the page. It demonstrates that the firm does not apply uniform advice. The contrast between a construction company and a payments operator is substantive — it names specific documents, specific regulations, and specific structural problems. This is not a placeholder comparison.

**Routing function:** Implicit. This section builds the case for why the visitor should go to a sector-specific Level 3 page rather than a generic contact form. It performs that routing logic in argumentative form rather than link form. This is the correct function for this section.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. The section describes the nature of the legal and structural work by industry without describing how engagements work, what they cost, or what specific services are included. It stays on the right side of the Services boundary.

**Tone:** The payments/MSB/PSP paragraph is particularly strong. Naming PCMLTFA, FINTRAC, and RPAA directly — rather than a vague "regulatory compliance" — demonstrates genuine sector literacy. This is a significant differentiator for ICP-02 visitors who can immediately assess whether the firm understands their world.

**Strategic flag:** None. Recommend this section be preserved as-is. The specificity of the regulatory references is an asset, not a liability, for this page's positioning.

---

### Section 4 — Common Industry-Specific Business and Legal Issues

**Coherence as Level 2:** PASS.
The issue lists are correctly framed as "recurring issues we see" rather than as legal advice or service offerings. The intro framing ("These are not emergencies — they are the structural and regulatory realities that operating businesses in each sector need to manage proactively") is a strong positioning statement that reinforces the preventative, structural orientation of the firm.

**Routing function:** Implicit. Each issue listed is a recognizable problem that a qualified visitor might see and think "that is exactly our situation." This creates the pull toward the more specific Level 3 page for their sector. The section does not need explicit routing links — its job is to create resonance, not to route directly.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. Issues are described as business problems, not as service descriptions. The distinction holds throughout.

**Tone:** PASS. The language is direct and structural. No fear language.

**Strategic observations:**

1. The Payments/Fintech/MSB/PSP section is the strongest issue list — six items, all specific, all naming concrete regulatory or commercial problems. This section will land with ICP-02 visitors.

2. The Food, Agriculture, and Consumer Goods section is the least developed. Four of the five issues listed (supplier agreements, distribution contracts, corporate governance, franchise law) apply to many industries. The section lacks a concrete differentiator that characterizes food/agriculture specifically. The editorial QA noted this as non-blocking; from a strategic standpoint, the same observation stands. The section performs adequately but has room to be made more sector-specific.

3. The framing "ownership transitions" appears in both the Construction section and the Energy and Logistics section. This repetition is not a problem at the section level (ownership transitions are genuinely relevant to both), but the two entries should use slightly different language at the production stage to avoid the impression of copy-paste.

**Strategic flag:** Minor. Food section and duplicate "ownership transitions" language are both non-blocking but recommended for revision at v2.

---

### Section 5 — Related Resources / Where To Go Next

**Coherence as Level 2:** PASS.
This section is the explicit routing mechanism. It correctly lists the Level 3 pages and tells visitors who each page is for. The fallback to Inquiries for visitors who do not find a category match is the right structural decision.

**Routing function:** STRONG. The section is clear and task-oriented. Each entry answers the question: "Which page is right for me?" in one sentence. The fallback to Inquiries closes the routing loop.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. The section links to Level 3 pages and to Inquiries — it does not duplicate either. The Inquiries link is positioned as a fallback, not as the primary conversion path, which correctly reflects POL-051's hierarchy.

**Tone:** Clean. The language is helpful and direct without being sales-oriented.

**Strategic observation:** The first entry ("Ontario Operating Companies — Corporate Structure and Governance") is broader than the industry-specific entries. This is a reasonable general ICP-01 routing option. The risk is that a visitor in one of the named industries (e.g., Construction) clicks the general Ontario entry rather than the construction-specific entry. At implementation, the visual design should make the industry-specific entries the primary options and position the general Ontario entry as a secondary option. Non-blocking at content level.

---

### Section 6 — Contact / CTA

**Coherence as Level 2:** STRONG.
The CTA section is exactly what a Level 2 pillar page CTA should be: two sentences that acknowledge the routing that just happened (the visitor should go to their specific Level 3 page if they found it) and provide a clean fallback path (Inquiries) for those who are ready to engage but have not identified a specific Level 3 destination.

**Routing function:** Clean. Routes to Level 3 (implicitly, through the prior section) or to Inquiries. No form. No conversion pressure.

**Alignment with Services / Inquiries boundary:** PASS. The CTA does not attempt to replace the Inquiries page — it correctly defers to it.

**Tone:** The second sentence names both ICP audiences explicitly: "Ontario operating businesses" and "payments or fintech operators with Canadian exposure." This is a strong final framing. It confirms to the right visitor that they are in the right place.

**Strategic flag:** None. This is a clean, restrained close.

## Consolidated Revision Recommendations (Non-Blocking)

These recommendations are for v2 consideration, subject to ML1 direction:

1. **Section 2 — Energy and Logistics descriptor:** Strengthen with one concrete differentiator (e.g., joint venture vehicles for project operations).
2. **Section 4 — Food, Agriculture, and Consumer Goods:** Add one or two more sector-specific issue points (e.g., food safety regulatory intersections, perishable supply chain contract considerations).
3. **Section 4 — "Ownership transitions" language:** Use distinct language in the Construction and Energy sections to avoid the impression of repeated copy.
4. **Section 5 — Ontario Operating Companies entry:** At implementation, distinguish visually between the general Ontario entry and the industry-specific entries to guide visitors to the more specific routing options first.

## What Must Not Change Before Publication

These elements are core to the page's strategic positioning and must be preserved:

- The construction vs. payments contrast in Section 1.
- The regulatory specificity (PCMLTFA, FINTRAC, RPAA) in Section 3.
- The ICP-02 industry entry (Payments/Fintech/MSB/PSP) in Section 2.
- The full Payments/Fintech/MSB/PSP issue list in Section 4.
- The restrained, non-conversion CTA in Section 6.
- The absence of a form, testimonials, or practice-area content anywhere on this page.
