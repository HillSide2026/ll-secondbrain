### Page 2 - Why This Matters

MSB classification is rarely just an administrative question.

For payments and crypto-payment operators, the classification analysis can shape
launch sequencing, banking access, AML program design, reporting obligations,
partner diligence, and how the product can scale.

The risk is not only that a company registers late. The larger operational risk
is building a product, onboarding partners, and raising capital around an
assumption that has never been tested against how funds or virtual currency
actually move.

This guide identifies six common product or operating patterns that should
trigger a closer MSB / foreign MSB classification review in Canada.

### Page 3 - How to Use This Guide

Use the six triggers as an issue-spotting tool, not as a final conclusion.

For each trigger, ask three questions:

1. Who gives the payment or transfer instruction?
2. Who receives, holds, transfers, exchanges, or makes available the funds or
   virtual currency?
3. Is the service being offered into Canada, operated from Canada, or directed
   at Canadian persons or entities?

If the answer is unclear, the next step is not a generic compliance checklist.
It is a flow-level classification analysis.

### Page 4 - Trigger 1: You Sit Between a Payer and a Payee

Many fintech products describe themselves as software, workflow tools, merchant
enablement, payroll infrastructure, or embedded finance.

The label matters less than the flow.

If the business receives payment instructions and acts as an intermediary
between a payer and a payee, the model may need MSB analysis. This is especially
important where the payer consents to make payment through the platform and the
payee has an agreement with the platform to access transfers.

Common examples:

- invoice payment platforms
- payroll or commission payment flows
- marketplace payout infrastructure
- embedded payment features inside SaaS products
- payment services for goods or services

The classification question is not whether the product feels like a money
transfer business. It is whether the business is performing a regulated money
movement function.

### Page 5 - Trigger 2: You Exchange Fiat and Virtual Currency

Virtual currency exchange activity is a classification trigger.

A business model should be reviewed where the product includes:

- funds to virtual currency
- virtual currency to funds
- virtual currency to virtual currency
- stablecoin conversion as part of a payment or settlement flow
- exchange functionality embedded inside a broader product

The stablecoin context matters. A platform may describe stablecoins as a
settlement tool rather than a crypto product. That may be commercially accurate,
but it does not remove the need to analyze whether exchange or transfer services
are being provided.

For Canadian purposes, the regulatory analysis follows the function, not the
branding.

### Page 6 - Trigger 3: You Transfer Virtual Currency for a Client

Virtual currency transfer services can create MSB exposure even where the
business never presents itself as an exchange.

Review the model where the business:

- transfers virtual currency at a client's request
- receives virtual currency for remittance to a beneficiary
- routes stablecoin settlement between counterparties
- supports on-chain settlement with off-chain payout
- controls or coordinates the transfer path in a customer-facing flow

This is a common issue in stablecoin payment infrastructure. Teams often focus
on custody, wallet design, and treasury mechanics. The classification question
may turn on who is requesting the transfer, who benefits, and what role the
platform performs in moving value.

### Page 7 - Trigger 4: You Are Outside Canada but Sell Into Canada

Canadian MSB analysis is not limited to companies incorporated in Canada.

A foreign business may need foreign MSB analysis where it:

- provides MSB services
- has no place of business in Canada
- directs those services at persons or entities in Canada
- provides those services to clients in Canada

Practical signals can include Canadian-facing marketing, a Canadian domain,
Canadian directory listings, Canadian customers, Canadian corridors, or product
materials that specifically invite Canadian use.

For international operators, the key point is simple: "we are not Canadian" is
not the end of the analysis.

### Page 8 - Trigger 5: You Rely on Agents, Mandataries, or Partner-Led Delivery

Agent and partner structures can solve distribution problems. They can also
create regulatory control problems.

Review the structure where:

- third parties deliver MSB services on your behalf
- Canadian agents or mandataries support onboarding or transaction activity
- a local partner is the visible customer-facing party
- responsibility for compliance is split across commercial agreements
- the operating model assumes the partner's registration covers the full flow

The question is not only who is registered. It is who performs the regulated
function, who controls the customer relationship, and how the obligations are
allocated in practice.

### Page 9 - Trigger 6: Your Bank or Partner Starts Asking Flow-Level Questions

A bank, sponsor, processor, or enterprise customer may identify the classification
issue before the company has formally named it.

Common signs:

- repeated questions about source of funds and destination of funds
- requests for transaction-flow diagrams
- questions about who owns customer funds at each step
- requests for AML policies, risk assessments, or monitoring procedures
- hesitation around stablecoin settlement or crypto-related counterparties
- partner review delays after volumes increase or use cases change

This does not always mean the company has an unresolved MSB issue. It does mean
the business model is now being assessed through a regulated-flow lens.

At that point, legal analysis has to meet the operating reality.

### Page 10 - What a Good Classification Review Looks Like

A useful review does not start with a conclusion.

It maps the operating model first:

- entities and jurisdictions
- customer types
- payment and virtual currency flows
- fiat entry and exit points
- custody and control points
- instructions and authorizations
- counterparties and banking partners
- Canadian nexus
- existing registrations and compliance infrastructure

Only after that map exists can counsel assess MSB, foreign MSB, RPAA, AML, and
adjacent regulatory questions with enough precision to be useful.

### Page 11 - The Operator's Working Checklist

Before seeking advice, collect:

- a one-page product description
- current and planned jurisdictions
- customer types and target markets
- flow of funds diagrams
- flow of virtual currency diagrams, if applicable
- screenshots or descriptions of the customer payment experience
- terms with users, merchants, payees, agents, and partners
- banking, processor, sponsor bank, or platform agreements
- current compliance program materials
- existing MSB, foreign MSB, PSP, or equivalent registrations
- questions already raised by banks, partners, investors, or regulators

The better the flow evidence, the better the classification answer.

## Source Notes

Public copy should not include long legal citations. These notes support ML1
review and future QA.

- FINTRAC states that MSBs operating in Canada and foreign MSBs directing and
  providing services to clients in Canada must register before operating.
- FINTRAC describes MSB services to include, among other services, foreign
  exchange dealing, remitting or transmitting funds, payment services for goods
  or services, invoice payment services, dealing in virtual currency, and
  crowdfunding platform services.
- FINTRAC describes dealing in virtual currency as including both virtual
  currency exchange services and virtual currency transfer services.
- FINTRAC describes foreign MSB criteria as providing MSB services, having no
  place of business in Canada, directing services at persons or entities in
  Canada, and providing services to clients in Canada.
- The Bank of Canada's RPAA materials remain relevant to separate PSP analysis:
  PSPs that perform payment functions related to electronic funds transfers and
  meet the statutory criteria may require Bank of Canada registration. RPAA
  analysis should remain distinct from FINTRAC MSB analysis.
