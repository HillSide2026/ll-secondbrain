# F3 ICP Profiles

Six profiles across the expanded F3 ICP: fintech operators building or running financial
infrastructure that enables movement, storage, access, or lending of money or value.
Securities dealers, investment advisers, portfolio managers, and token issuers are
out of scope.

---

## Profile 1 — The Canadian Fintech Founder

**Name:** Dani Osei
**Title:** Co-Founder & CEO
**Company:** Series A cross-border payroll platform, Toronto. 35 employees. Processes
CAD/USD/GHS corridors. Registered MSB.

**Problem set:**
She built the product. Now the compliance layer is catching up to her. She has an MSB
registration but isn't sure if she's fully inside RPAA or just adjacent to it. Her bank
is asking questions she doesn't have clean answers to. Her CTO keeps asking why the
Canadian corridors are slower than the US ones and she can't explain it technically.

**Daily frustrations:**
- Her US-based investors don't understand why Canadian expansion is slower or more
  expensive than they modeled
- FINTRAC compliance feels like a black box maintained by a part-time contractor
- She can't find a lawyer who speaks payments operator — everyone she's tried gives
  her securities law disclaimers when she asks about stablecoins as a settlement option
- Her bank relationship manager changed twice in 18 months

**What she reads:**
BetaKit, Fintech Nexus archive, LinkedIn (curated toward Canadian fintech founders),
Payments NEXT when something gets shared

**What she googles:**
- "RPAA registration requirements Canada"
- "MSB vs PSP Canada difference"
- "can a Canadian MSB use USDC for settlement"
- "FINTRAC compliance checklist 2026"
- "bank account for fintech Canada"

## Profile 2 — The Cross-Border Expansion VP

**Name:** James Whitfield
**Title:** VP, Market Expansion
**Company:** US-based B2B payments platform, Series C, San Francisco. Processes
USD/EUR/MXN. Preparing Canadian market entry. No Canadian entity yet.

**Problem set:**
He's been given Canada as his next market. On paper it looked straightforward —
English-speaking, adjacent, similar economy. In practice, every assumption is wrong.
Interac doesn't work the way he expected. His compliance team flagged RPAA but nobody
internally can scope it. His general counsel is a US firm with no Canadian capability.
Legal review is adding 6+ weeks to every decision.

**Daily frustrations:**
- The internal timeline to Canada was 90 days. It's now 7 months.
- He keeps getting advice calibrated to US regulatory structure that doesn't port to Canada
- He can't get a straight answer on whether his current architecture needs to be
  restructured or just registered
- His CEO is asking why Canada is taking longer than Mexico did

**What he reads:**
PYMNTS, Fintech Blueprint newsletter, LinkedIn (follows US payments operators and VCs),
occasional BetaKit when Canada-specific results surface

**What he googles:**
- "payments regulation Canada vs US"
- "RPAA registration who needs to register"
- "Canadian payments market entry checklist"
- "Interac for US businesses"
- "RTR Canada launch date"
- "cross-border ACH equivalent Canada"

## Profile 3 — The Stablecoin Infrastructure Operator

**Name:** Omar Aziz
**Title:** Founder & CTO
**Company:** Seed-stage stablecoin infrastructure startup, Dubai/remote. Building
USDC-based settlement rails for SME cross-border payments. Targeting North America
and MENA corridors.

**Problem set:**
His product is technically built. He's using stablecoins as the settlement layer —
USDC in, local fiat out. He needs to understand which jurisdictions he can operate
in without triggering registration requirements. Canada came up because a potential
enterprise customer is Canadian. He has no idea whether his architecture classifies
as an MSB under the PCMLTFA or falls under the RPAA or both — and he can't find a
single piece of content that explains it without immediately turning into a sales pitch.

**Daily frustrations:**
- Most payments lawyers treat "stablecoin" as a synonym for "crypto investment product"
  and immediately route him to securities counsel
- He can't find clear operational guidance on where the MSB classification line sits
  for his specific architecture
- Banking relationships are his biggest blocker — three banks have passed on him
  without explanation
- His investors are asking him to map regulatory exposure before the next round, and
  he doesn't know how to scope it

**What he reads:**
Stablecoin Insider, Fintech Blueprint, a16z crypto blog, Twitter/X (payments infra
and stablecoin settlement threads), occasional PYMNTS

**What he googles:**
- "stablecoin payment operator Canada regulation"
- "USDC settlement MSB registration Canada"
- "RPAA stablecoin applicability"
- "crypto payment company bank account Canada"
- "PCMLTFA virtual currency dealer definition"

## Profile 4 — The Compliance Head at an Established MSB

**Name:** Kevin Tran
**Title:** Chief Compliance Officer
**Company:** Mid-size Canadian MSB, Vancouver. Processes CAD/USD/PHP/VND corridors.
~$200M annual volume. 12 years operating. 8-person compliance team.

**Problem set:**
He's been doing this a long time. He knows FINTRAC. His problem is that the regulatory
surface is expanding faster than his team. RPAA is new. The RTR is coming. His bank
is flagging transaction patterns that his AML program wasn't designed to flag. His CEO
wants to add a stablecoin settlement option and Kevin has no framework for scoping that
risk. He needs external counsel but doesn't want a firm that will run a discovery process
he has to manage.

**Daily frustrations:**
- Outside counsel he's used gives him hedged, over-broad opinions that take 6 weeks
  and don't answer the operational question
- FINTRAC guidance is written for compliance teams, not for product teams — translating
  it for his product lead is a recurring task he resents
- His bank relationship is stable but fragile — one examiner question answered wrong
  and it could change
- He's the only person in his company who understands the regulatory picture and that
  means everything lands on him

**What he reads:**
FINTRAC guidance bulletins, OSFI advisories, ACAMS publications, LinkedIn (follows
other compliance professionals and payments regulatory accounts), BetaKit occasionally

**What he googles:**
- "RPAA vs MSB Canada overlap"
- "FINTRAC STR threshold 2026"
- "stablecoin AML obligations Canada"
- "RTR compliance requirements PSP"
- "bank de-risking MSB Canada"

## Profile 5 — The Embedded Finance Operator

**Name:** Sara Okonkwo
**Title:** Co-Founder & CEO
**Company:** Series B embedded finance platform, Toronto. Offers financial accounts,
card programs, and payment flows to SME customers via a partner bank. 80 employees.
Not yet independently licensed.

**Problem set:**
Her product works. Her partner bank works. The question is how long that remains true.
As volumes grow, her partner bank is asking harder questions about her customer base
and transaction patterns. She's starting to think about whether she needs her own MSB
registration, her own RPAA analysis, or whether her program manager structure insulates
her. She doesn't know where the line is.

**Daily frustrations:**
- Her partner bank's compliance team changes requirements without notice
- She doesn't know if she's operating in a grey zone on MSB classification
- Card network rules and banking rules create conflicting obligations she can't
  easily reconcile
- Her investors want her to expand to the US and she doesn't know if her Canadian
  compliance structure is portable

**What she reads:**
Fintech Blueprint, BetaKit, a16z fintech newsletter, LinkedIn (follows BaaS and
embedded finance founders)

**What she googles:**
- "embedded finance MSB registration Canada"
- "BaaS program manager compliance requirements Canada"
- "do I need MSB registration if I use a partner bank"
- "RPAA embedded finance applicability"
- "card program compliance Canada fintech"

## Profile 6 — The Crypto Wallet / Custody Operator

**Name:** David Lim
**Title:** COO
**Company:** Mid-size Canadian crypto custody and wallet platform, Vancouver.
MSB-registered. Serves institutional and high-net-worth clients. Holds BTC, ETH,
and stablecoins in custody. 40 employees. FINTRAC-compliant program in place.

**Problem set:**
He's past the registration question — that's done. His problems are operational and
forward-looking. Banking relationships are stable but brittle. FINTRAC examination
appetite is increasing. He needs to understand how RPAA applies to his custody
operations specifically. He's watching CSA and CIRO make moves that don't technically
apply to him yet but will reshape his operating environment. He needs counsel who
understands his specific model — not a generalist who starts every engagement by
explaining what a virtual currency dealer is.

**Daily frustrations:**
- Most lawyers treat him as a regulatory problem rather than an operating business
- FINTRAC compliance guidance is written at a level of abstraction that doesn't
  answer his actual operational questions
- Banking relationships require constant maintenance and documentation that takes
  real time
- He can't get a clear answer on how RPAA requirements interact with his existing
  MSB registration for custody specifically
- Institutional clients are asking for compliance documentation he's not sure how
  to structure

**What he reads:**
CoinDesk (regulatory coverage), Stablecoin Insider, FINTRAC enforcement notices,
OSC/CSA updates, LinkedIn (follows Canadian crypto regulatory accounts)

**What he googles:**
- "crypto custody MSB Canada compliance"
- "RPAA wallet custody operator applicability"
- "banking for crypto custody platform Canada"
- "FINTRAC virtual currency dealer examination 2026"
- "institutional crypto custody regulatory requirements Canada"
